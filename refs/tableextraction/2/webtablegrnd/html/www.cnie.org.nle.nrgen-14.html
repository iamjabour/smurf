Summary

The U.S. Department of Agriculture's (USDA) Farm Service Administration (FSA) conducted the 15th enrollment period for the conservation Reserve Program(CRP) during March 1997. During this period, farmers and landowners submitted more than 300,000 bids to retire more than 25 million acres of highly erodible and environmentally sensitive cropland from production. USDA has stated that it plans to accept bids for 19 million acres, and to announce the successful bidders in June. This report includes a table listing, by state, the:

· Number of bids, or offers, received;

· Total acres offered for enrollment;

· Acres offered that are currently enrolled in the CRP;

· Acres offered are not currently enrolled in the CRP;

· Acres on which contracts expire on September 30, 1997;

· Percentage of acres currently in the program that were offered for reenrollment; and

· Percentage of acres offered that are not currently enrolled in the CRP.

For background information about the CRP, including its history and policy issues as USDA implements program revisions enacted in the 1996 farm bill(P.L. 104-127), see CRS Report 96-760 ENR, Conservation Reserve Program: Status and Policy Issues, updated on February 24, 1997.

Background

The CRP, enacted in 1985, enables producers and landowners to bid to retire highly erodible or environmentally sensitive cropland from production. Land is usually retired for 10 years, but the contract can be longer under certain circumstances. Participants receive annual rental and cost-sharing payments, and technical assistance to install approved plantings. The Farm Service Administration administers the program, while the Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS) provides technical assistance.

The program has proven very popular. Up to 36.4 million acres have been enrolled, making this the largest long-term agricultural land retirement program. (The total acreage now in the program is probably around 32 million acres.) Program accomplishments include reducing erosion, boosting crop prices, stabilizing land prices, enhancing wildlife habitat, and providing other environmental benefits, such as water quality protection. The initial contracts started to expire in 1995, and contracts on almost 22 million acres will expire on September 30, 1997.

Congress reauthorized and revised the CRP in the 1996 farm bill (P .L. 104-127). It required FSA to issue revised rules 90 days after enactment, which would have been early July, 1996. But the rule-making process was difficult, and FSA issued draft rules in September and the final rule became effective on February 12, 1997. In March, the FSA held a signup under these new rules, the 15th in the program's history. Bids in this signup could not exceed average county land rental rates, adjusted for soil quality, which were published and available to all bidders. FSA will rank and compare all bids using an environmental benefits index. The index awards points for the following variables:

· Wildlife habitat benefits (up to 100 points);

· Water quality benefits (up to 100 points);

· On-farm benefits from reducing erosion (up to 100 points);

· Likely long-term benefits beyond the contact period, such as from planting trees (up to 50 points);

· Air quality benefits from reduced wind erosion (up to 25 points);

· Benefits of enrollment in conservation priority areas to help improve adverse water quality, wildlife habitat, or air quality (up to 25 points);and

· Cost to enroll the acres (to be determined after the signup period).

Bidders accumulating the most points will be accepted into the program. FSA will be able to determine the cutoff level only after it has arrayed all the bids. The Department announced that it plans to limit accepted bids to 19 million acres, so not all bids, which include more than 25 million acres in aggregate, can be accepted. It seems likely that a few of the bids to reenroll land that is currently in the program, totalling more than 18 million acres, will be unsuccessful. Various interests pressed for preferred enrollment patterns in the rule making process and will be seeking these same patterns from the enrollment process. The patterns they seek range from reenrolling all current contracts, to getting more land into the program east of the Mississippi, to making sure than certain kinds of benefits receive more emphasis in the future.

The 15th Enrollment--A Preliminary Assessment

As shown on Table 1, more than 300,000 bids were offered to enroll more than 25.6 million acres. States with more than 20,000 bids include Iowa, Kansas, Minnesota, and North Dakota. States with more that 2 million acres offered include Texas, North Dakota, Montana, and Kansas. The most striking feature of these statistics is how similar they are, as a percentage, for the states with large acreage already in the program. In states with large numbers of participating acres, the portion offered for reenrollment varies by less than 15% (from 77% in Missouri to 90% in North Dakota and Texas). The percentages for land offered that are not currently in the CRP are somewhat broader (ranging from 15% in Kansas and Washington to 57% in South Dakota). These data may mask significant differences between current enrollment and land bid to enroll in this signup within states. The degree to which the benefits of the new mix of acres within a state will be different from the current mix can not be determined from available information.

A large majority of the acres currently in the program were offered for reenrollment, 84% nationwide. Almost all the states with large acreage in the program offered very similar percentages for reenrollment. A notable exception is South Dakota, which offered less than 80% for reenrollment, while adjoining wheat-growing states generally offered close to 90%, on average. Generally, lower percentages were offered by states east of the Mississippi River and states that have small acreage in the program, but there were exceptions to both of these generalizations, such as New Mexico (96%), Wyoming (89%), and West Virginia (83%). Southeastern states with large acreage in the program generally offered about 80% of currently enrolled acres, while Mississippi Delta states offered slightly less, except for Mississippi, which offered 87%. Acres that are not reenrolled are eligible to return to production; if they are highly erodible, they must meet conservation compliance requirements or the operators risk losing many federal farm program benefits.

Nationwide, 30% of the land offered was not already enrolled. As noted above, most states with larger acreage in the program were close to the nationwide average, and the larger deviations from that average are almost all states with limited enrolled acreage. States with significant agricultural acres where larger portions of new acres were bid included South Dakota (57%), Louisiana (48%), North Dakota (47%), Ohio (43%), and Michigan (41%). Some of the states with low percentages in new acres included Tennessee (9%), Kansas (15%), and Washington (15%).

Some environmental interests had hoped that larger acreage would be bid in the eastern United States, providing additional water quality benefits. Indiana, Illinois, Ohio and Michigan were all above the national average, with between 35% and 43% new acreage offered. But these acreage figures and percentages alone do not indicate whether new and additional benefits would result. States in the northeast and mid-Atlantic bid to enroll very few acres, and bids from the two states with the most acres in the program in these regions, Pennsylvania and Virginia, included only 28% and 9% in new acres, respectively. Another reason that it is difficult to discern changes in the mix of benefits is that the program, as revised, also allows for continuous enrollment of lands that provide very high environmental values, such as stream buffers. Continuous enrollment has been available for less than a year, and the data in table 1 do not include lands signed up under this effort. Reportedly, enrollment under continuous signup has been limited because FSA staff have been engaged in trying to complete the rule-writing and administer the 15th signup.

Issues of Current Interest to Congress

Congress has been very interested in all aspects of the CRP, as revised by the 1996 farm bill. The House Agriculture Committee's subcommittee concerned with conservation held oversight hearings on September 19, 1996, and again on February 26, 1997. CRP participants receive almost $2 billion per year in payments. Several issues have come up at oversight hearings or been raised in other forums by Members, including signup procedures, how acreage is prioritized, and how bids will be compared. These may resurface again when the Department announces which bids have been accepted and rejected.

In recent weeks, two issues relating to the 15th signup have received considerable attention. The first is how the schedule for announcing successful bids might affect winter wheat growers who will need to making planting decisions for next year. If the announcement is delayed until later in the summer, those producers who do not get into the CRP may not have time to readjust and plant a crop for the coming year. While planting would not occur until early fall, these producers are concerned that any delay, and even an announcement in June, would not give them enough time to complete the necessary site preparations if their bids are not accepted and they decide to return their land to production. This concern has prompted legislation (H.R. 1342), drafted by the House Agriculture Committee leadership to be budget-neutral, that would allow a 1 year extension of contracts for land on which wheat, barley, or oats is planted. The intention of supporters is to create a seamless process for these producers, whether their bids are accepted or rejected. How others might react to such a proposal, and enactment are open questions.

A second issue was raised when the Clinton Administration proposed recently to delay enrollment of 2 million acres for a year, and to use the money saved to purchase mineral rights from a proposed mining operation near Yellowstone National Park. This purchase would fulfill a Clinton Administration campaign promise to end the potential environmental threat of mining at this location. It has not been received well by agricultural interests, who believe that CRP funds should not be diverted to this unrelated proposal, and suggest the Land and Water Conservation Fund is a more appropriate source. With the large size of the bidding pool for the 15th signup, it would mean that 2 million acres of bids waiting to be accepted this time would not get into the program and would have to wait for future opportunities. This proposal must be approved by Congress, and agricultural leaders in both Chambers have indicated in public forums that they will oppose it.
