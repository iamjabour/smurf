Note on Change in Accounting Practice:

Financial results in this report reflect the Securities and Exchange Commission's Staff Accounting Bulletin (SAB) No. 101 on revenue recognition.

In accordance with SAB No. 101, for the year 2000 and ongoing the company delays recognition of revenue for certain shipments until they physically reach the customer. The only shipments affected are those whose terms call for title to pass at point of delivery. All other shipments continue to be recognized under TI's historical accounting practice, which is recognition upon shipment.

The result of this change in accounting practice is that fourth-quarter revenue was $33 million, or 1.1 percent, less than would have been recognized under the prior accounting method and fourth-quarter pro forma income was $11 million, or $0.006 per share, less. For the year, pro forma revenue was $41 million, or 0.3 percent, less and pro forma income was $14 million, or $0.008 per share, less.

Quarterly income statement information for 2000 inclusive of this accounting change is available on the company's web site at: http://www.ti.com/corp/docs/investor/fin/proforma.shtml



DALLAS, Jan. 22 -- Texas Instruments Incorporated (NYSE: TXN) today reported record pro forma annual revenue and profits for the year 2000. Revenue was $11,861 million, up 22 percent from 1999's $9759 million. Earnings per share were $1.22, up 36 percent from $0.90 in 1999.

In the fourth quarter of 2000, TI's semiconductor revenue grew 18 percent over the year-ago quarter, and was even with semiconductor revenue in the third quarter. Semiconductor revenue growth in the fourth quarter was affected by a combination of higher inventories at customers and lower consumption rates of electronic end-equipment. As weakening in their own business environments became evident, customers responded by adjusting their demand, which significantly impacted TI's semiconductor revenue and orders late in the quarter.

``In 2000, TI delivered some of the best financial results in its history, including record pro forma revenue and profit, and a strong cash position,'' said Tom Engibous, TI chairman, president and CEO. ``The year also was a milestone in strengthening our strategic position in DSP and Analog. Most notable was TI's acquisition of Burr-Brown, which made TI one of the premier players in high-performance Analog. Additionally, TI emerged as one of the fastest-growing providers of DSL and cable modem chipsets, and catalog products became one of the most important contributors to TI's revenue growth by increasing half a billion dollars, or 47 percent.

``DSP and Analog continue to be the core semiconductor drivers of Internet-age electronics, despite a weaker economic environment that is resulting in slower near-term demand for our customers' products and, therefore, for our own,'' Engibous said. ``We will maintain our strategic R&D investments to further build our leadership in DSP and Analog -- both in the broad market for our catalog products as well as in a variety of high-volume end-equipment markets. At the same time, we will continue tight control of operating and overhead expenses. As excess inventories are absorbed and we return to a stronger growth environment, TI will be able to quickly capitalize on opportunities.''

Note: TI's financial results reflect the completed pooling of interests acquisition of Burr-Brown Corporation and the inclusion of its results in the semiconductor segment. Historical results have been restated to reflect Burr-Brown's operations. Pro forma information excludes certain costs, charges and gains. For more information, see financial statements.

Summary of Financial Results

  • Pro forma revenue for TI was $3033 million, up 15 percent from $2638 million in the year-ago quarter due to strength in semiconductor, and down 3 percent sequentially due to seasonally lower revenue in Educational and Productivity Solutions (E&PS). Semiconductor revenue was $2695 million, up 18 percent from $2285 million in the same quarter of 1999, and even with $2692 million in the third quarter of 2000 as a decline in DSP revenue from wireless and catalog offset growth in Analog and other DSP products.
  • Pro forma operating profit was $683 million, an increase of 17 percent from $584 million in the year-ago quarter due to increased product shipments in semiconductor. Pro forma operating profit declined 8 percent from the third quarter of 2000, due to seasonal declines in E&PS.
  • Pro forma operating margin was 22.5 percent, up 0.4 percentage points from the year-ago quarter as lower selling, general & administrative (SG&A) expenses more than offset increased research & development (R&D) investments in DSP and Analog. Pro forma operating margin declined 1.2 percentage points from the third quarter of 2000 due to seasonal declines in E&PS.
  • Pro forma income for the quarter increased 22 percent to $549 million, compared with $448 million in the year-ago quarter, as higher interest income extended the gains from operating activities. Pro forma income declined 7 percent from the third quarter of 2000.
  • Pro forma earnings per share (EPS) increased to $0.31 from $0.25 in the fourth quarter of 1999, and declined $0.02 from the third quarter of 2000.
  • Orders in the fourth quarter were $2772 million, down 2 percent from the year-ago quarter. Sequentially, orders were down 16 percent due to semiconductor.
  • According to generally accepted accounting principles, for the fourth quarter of 2000: revenue was $3033 million, compared with $2638 million in the year-ago quarter; operating profit was $615 million, compared with $503 million; operating margin was 20.3 percent, compared with 19.0 percent; net income was $665 million, compared with $445 million; and diluted earnings per share were $0.37, compared with $0.25.

Summary of 2000 Financial Results

  • Pro forma revenue was $11,861 million, up 22 percent from $9759 million in 1999 due to growth in semiconductor. Semiconductor revenue was $10,284 million, up 25 percent from $8231 million in 1999 due to gains in Analog and DSP.
  • Pro forma operating profit was $2702 million, an increase of 36 percent from $1986 million in 1999 due to higher semiconductor profits.
  • Pro forma operating margin was 22.8 percent, up 2.4 percentage points from 1999 as revenue growth outpaced increases in cost of goods sold and SG&A expenses.
  • Pro forma income for the year increased 38 percent to $2174 million, compared with $1581 million in 1999.
  • Pro forma EPS increased to $1.22, compared with $0.90 in 1999.
  • Orders in 2000 were $12,405 million, up 20 percent from the year earlier due to gains in semiconductor.
  • According to generally accepted accounting principles, for 2000: revenue was $11,875 million, compared with $9759 million in the prior year; operating profit was $2339 million, compared with $1755 million; operating margin was 19.7 percent, compared with 18.0 percent; net income was $3058 million, compared with $1451 million; and diluted earnings per share were $1.71, compared with $0.83.

Outlook

TI expects its revenue to decline about 10 percent sequentially in the first quarter as semiconductor customers continue to work through excess inventory during this seasonally slower period. The weakening world economy limits visibility into market conditions for the remainder of the year.

Specifically, TI expects the following for the first quarter:

  • Revenue from wireless will decline sequentially as certain handset customers continue to absorb semiconductor inventory.
  • Revenue from catalog DSP and Analog products is expected to be about even with fourth quarter.
  • Revenue from broadband communications should continue to grow, reflecting strong market acceptance of TI's products.
  • Revenue from TI's non-semiconductor activities, including Sensors & Controls (formerly Materials & Controls) and E&PS, is expected to be about even with fourth quarter.
  • Pro forma operating margin is expected to decline five to six points as a result of lower revenue.
  • Pro forma non-operating income is expected to decline to about $70 million primarily due to lower interest and investment income.

    For 2001:

  • R&D is expected to be $1.7 billion pro forma, compared with $1.6 billion in 2000.
  • Capital expenditures are expected to be $2.3 billion, compared with $2.8 billion in 2000, for manufacturing equipment for TI's DSP and Analog products, including equipment for TI's initial 300-millimeter wafer fabrication facility in Dallas.
  • Depreciation is expected to increase to $1.5 billion, compared with $1.2 billion in 2000.
  • The pro forma tax rate is expected to be 31 percent, the same as 2000.

Semiconductor

  • Semiconductor revenue in the fourth quarter was $2695 million, up 18 percent from the same period in 1999 due to growth in Analog product revenue, and even with the third quarter. For the year, semiconductor revenue was $10,284 million, up 25 percent from 1999 due to growth in Analog and DSP products.
  • Semiconductor operating profit for the fourth quarter was $679 million, or 25.2 percent of revenue, compared with $577 million in the year-ago period and $681 million in the third quarter. For the year, operating profit grew 38 percent to $2607 million.
  • Fourth-quarter Analog revenue grew 29 percent, compared with the year-ago period and 6 percent sequentially. For the year, Analog revenue increased 28 percent. DSP revenue in the fourth quarter decreased 4 percent from the same quarter a year ago and 13 percent sequentially. For the year, DSP revenue grew 29 percent. For the year, Analog and DSP comprised about 65 percent of TI's semiconductor revenue.
  • TI's remaining semiconductor revenue increased in the fourth quarter from the year-ago quarter and was about even with the third quarter of 2000. Revenue also increased for the year.
  • TI's semiconductor revenue in key markets was as follows:
    • Wireless revenue declined 7 percent in the fourth quarter versus the year-ago period and 15 percent sequentially. For the year, wireless revenue increased more than $500 million, or 28 percent, to $2.3 billion.
    • Revenue from TI's catalog DSP and Analog products grew 34 percent in the fourth quarter versus the year-ago period and declined 6 percent sequentially. For the year, catalog revenue increased more than $500 million, or 47 percent, to $1.6 billion.
    • Growth in broadband communications, including DSL and cable modems, continued to accelerate in the fourth quarter with revenue doubling sequentially. For the year, broadband revenue grew to about $150 million from a negligible amount in 1999.
  • Fourth-quarter semiconductor orders were even with the year-ago quarter, and declined 15 percent sequentially primarily due to declines in wireless and catalog products. For the year, orders increased 23 percent.

Semiconductor Highlights for the Year

  • TI announced two new DSP cores that further extend its technology leadership. The TMS320C64x™ core will have 10 times better performance than the previous leader, TI's TMS320C62x™ core. The C64x targets broadband infrastructure applications. The TMS320C55x™ core targets portable electronic devices such as wireless phones. The first DSP device powered by the C55x core (TMS320C5510™) was delivered in June.
  • During the year, TI announced four strategic acquisitions strengthening its position in DSP and Analog:
    • Burr-Brown extends TI's Analog position into new high-performance data converters and amplifiers, and increases TI's ability to offer its DSP customers essentially all of the Analog components that attach to a DSP.
    • Dot Wireless' technology leadership in CDMA for the emerging IS-2000 third-generation (3G) cellular standard will enable TI to establish strong product positions across the full spectrum of 3G standards.
    • Alantro Communications specializes in wireless local area networking technology using the emerging IEEE 802.11 standard that will distribute broadband information throughout the home, including video, data and voice.
    • Toccata Technology provides TI with digital audio amplifier technology, a critical component in TI's December introduction of an all-digital audio solution with concert-hall quality.
  • TI's Open Multimedia Application Platform (OMAP™) software platform already has gained significant momentum by becoming the platform of choice by major OEMs, including Nokia, Ericsson, Sony and Handspring, for their next-generation wireless data products. Wireless data will be used for a host of information and entertainment applications, including Internet audio (MP3), mobile-commerce and streaming video. By working with Microsoft, Symbian and Sun, TI has opened the OMAP platform to innovative application development by the WinCE, EPOC and Java software developer communities.
  • TI's DSL chipset solutions enabled more than 3 million ports in 2000. An additional 8 million ports included TI's DSL Analog technology. Three out of the top four central office manufacturers and four out of the top six personal computer makers are engaged with TI. Through Actiontec Electronics, TI DSL technology now is being deployed in the retail channel, making TI the first silicon provider to help make DSL modems available through nationwide retail outlets.
  • After less than a year and a half in the cable modem business, TI believes it has garnered about 30 percent of the market for data over cable service interface specification (DOCSIS) chips, delivering 3 million of the nearly 10 million total units estimated to have shipped in 2000.
  • TI introduced more than 350 new catalog analog products in 2000. TI had more than 15,000 design-ins for its catalog analog products, almost four times the number of design-ins from last year. Catalog DSP design-ins almost doubled over 1999 and the sale of tools required to write software for TI DSPs increased almost 150 percent during the year.

Sensors & Controls

The name and financial results of TI's Materials & Controls segment have been adjusted to reflect the sale of its specialty-clad metals and electrical contacts (or "materials") operation. The sale closed on November 13, 2000, and resulted in a gain. Financial results for the divested operation have been excluded from Sensors & Controls and included in "Divested Activities".

  • Revenue in the fourth quarter was $250 million, up 9 percent from the year-ago quarter, primarily due to gains in sensor products. Sequentially, fourth-quarter revenue was up 2 percent. For the year, revenue increased 13 percent to $1030 million, primarily due to gains in sensor product revenue, including the effect of a third-quarter 1999 acquisition.
  • Operating profit in the fourth quarter was $42 million, or 16.9 percent of revenue, compared with $37 million in the year-ago period and $43 million in the third quarter. For the year, operating profit grew 26 percent to $191 million.
Educational & Productivity Solutions (E&PS)

  • Revenue for E&PS in the fourth quarter of 2000 was $66 million, down 27 percent from $90 million in the year-ago quarter due to weak retail demand. Sequentially, revenue declined 62 percent primarily due to seasonality. For the year, revenue declined 8 percent to $446 million from $484 million due to the weak retail environment.
  • Operating profit for the quarter was $6 million, the same as the year-ago quarter, and down from $64 million in the third quarter primarily due to seasonality. Operating margin was 9.6 percent, compared with 6.8 percent in the year-ago quarter, and 36.3 percent in the third quarter of 2000. For the year, operating profit was $111 million, or 24.9 percent of revenue, compared with $106 million in 1999.
