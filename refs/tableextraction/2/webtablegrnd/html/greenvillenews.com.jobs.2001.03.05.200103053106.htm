SCORE tips give leg up to start-ups



STAFF/WIRE REPORTS



With more and more companies projecting lower profits in a slower economy, SCORE — the Service Corps of Retired Executives — has some tips for those looking to break out on their own.





Find your talents. Identify your life's passion and think of it in terms of profit. It may be product-oriented or service-oriented.





Confer with your colleagues. Talk with mentors, friends and family about your start-up ideas. You'll want their support or suggestions before you dive into the business. You can tap the help of SCORE counselors, too.





Do your research. Learn the ins and outs of the business by attending a few association meetings, local networking groups or by talking with business owners in similar fields. Research the industry on the Internet and investigate the competition.





Money — before and after. Analyze your financial resources before you sink your savings into your business venture. Identify your business start-up and ongoing costs. Determine how much you'll need to support yourself or your family — if your venture isn't an instant success, where will you find grocery money?





Keep records. Com- plete, accurate records are needed to file taxes and properly manage your bank accounts. Also record business transactions, new business ideas and business mistakes. All these records can provide guidance in the future.





Family life. Discuss the possible lifestyle, financial and emotional changes your family will encounter. Bring all concerns out in the open and make sure everyone is behind your decision.





Do a self-appraisal. Evaluate your personal strengths and weaknesses — motivation, organization, stamina, etc.





Resources. Decide where you will find the capital, equipment, employees and vendors you'll need. Be sure to have backups.





Remember your reasons. Before starting a business, write down why you elected to start your business. Keep that in mind at all times.



SCORE points out its counselors are available as sounding boards and to give advice, either face to face or via e-mail. Like other experienced business owners, SCORE counselors can assist with business planning and forecasting.



The SCORE Association was formed in 1964 as a nonprofit association dedicated to entrepreneur education and the formation, growth and success of small business nationwide.



SCORE has an office at 300 E. Washington St., Greenville. Its phone number is 271-3638.
