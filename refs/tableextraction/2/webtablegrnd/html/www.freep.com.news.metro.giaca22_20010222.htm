Mob figure in hospital and called near death

Associates report Giacalone has heart and kidney problems

February 22, 2001

BY JOE SWICKARD

FREE PRESS STAFF WRITER

Reputed Mafia captain Anthony (Tony Jack) Giacalone -- the man who many believe holds the answer to Jimmy Hoffa's disappearance -- is gravely ill at St. John Hospital in Detroit, according to close associates.

Giacalone, under federal indictment on racketeering charges, has been hospitalized with heart failure, in addition to complications from a kidney condition that requires frequent dialysis, the associates said.

"He's a fine gentleman and he's always been a fighter," said attorney William Bufalino II. "I'm sure he'll beat this, too."

Long feared as a ruthless enforcer of organized crime's rules, with an icy, intimidating stare, Giacalone has been a frail figure in recent years. He was recovering from heart surgery when he was named in the 1996 Mafia conspiracy indictment along with his bother Vito (Billy Jack) Giacalone.

Hospital spokesperson Zoila Brown confirmed that Giacalone, 82, of Clinton Township was a patient Wednesday, but said that his family requested that no information about his condition be released.

Giacalone's federal trial has been delayed for months because of his precarious health. That trial, of him and Anthony Zerilli, was expected to be the capstone of a federal Organized Crime Strike Force sweep of Detroit's Mafia leadership.

"It seems to be the consensus of opinion that he's in pretty bad shape," said Keith Corbett, head of the force's prosecution team.

Listed as one of Detroit's top 10 organized crime figures in U.S. Senate testimony, Giacalone was seen as a key player in the Hoffa mystery.

Hoffa was supposed to meet Giacalone at the Machus Red Fox Restaurant in Bloomfield Township when he disappeared in 1975. At the time, Giacalone was making an uncharacteristically high-profile visit to the Southfield Athletic Club. When told that Hoffa was missing, he mused, "Maybe he took a little trip."

The target of repeated investigations, Giacalone was convicted of tax evasion in 1976, drawing a 10-year sentence.

His reputation as a fastidious dresser was enhanced in the tax trial with testimony about his wardrobe of 300 custom-made suits.
