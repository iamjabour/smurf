New Concerns Over FBI Net Spy Service

New FBI Carnivore documents raise 4th Amendment concerns among public and in the U.S. Congress.

Disclosures by the U.S. Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) concerning its embattled "Carnivore Diagnostic Tool" sparked doubts Thursday about past assurances that the technology will not violate Fourth Amendment rights against "unreasonable search and seizure."

The Electronic Privacy Information Center (EPIC), the organization demanding disclosure of the once-classified documents under the Freedom of Information Act (FOIA), denounced the intitial batch presented October 2nd for failing to include Carnivore's source code. EPIC also said that the FBI failed to provide all the information required by the FOIA decree.

Thursday's disclosures, though sparse in detail, nevertheless alarmed privacy advocates, who are adamant that the FBI make all of Carnivore's capabilities public.

"The little information that has become public raises serious questions about the privacy implications of this technology," EPIC general counsel David Sobel said. "The American public cannot be expected to accept an Internet snooping system that is veiled in secrecy."

Omnivorous Ancestors

According to news sources and the FBI, the Carnivore system intercepts data packets sent over the Internet in order to gather evidence against those under federal investigation. Its original incarnation, code-named "Omnivore," ran on a Solaris X86 computer from early 1997 through 1999, when it was succeeded by Carnivore, which operates on a Windows NT terminal.

FBI officials have stated that Carnivore is already an integral component of their security operations. Noting that terrorists and other criminals are increasingly turning to the Internet, the FBI stresses the need to maintain vigilance against such threats.

Bureau officials have repeatedly pledged that the technology strictly abides by current electronic surveillance laws, and is used solely to collect hard evidence against suspects. They allege that filters within the system prevent the system from being used as a random intelligence-gathering device.

AKA "Jaws"

However, EPIC's Sobel told news sources, "The information in today's packet seems to conflict with what the FBI said about how Carnivore collects information that's been filtered."

Sobel referred to a document dated June 5, 2000, that EPIC has since posted on its Web site.

"Carnivore was tested on a real world deployment [deletion] having recently come back from a deployment... This PC [running Carnivore] could reliably capture and archive all unfiltered traffic to the internal hard drive at [deleted]," the heavily edited document stated.

"I always go into this process of getting these documents with the assumption that they're really going to raise questions and not give us answers," said Sobel. "But this clearly gives us some more questions."

Opposition Increases

"This is a very important issue, both in terms of public accountability and as a legal issue," EPIC Director Marc Rotenberg told the E-Commerce Times last August.

The U.S. Congress apparently agrees. Fears over Carnivore's capacity to capture e-mail and related Internet traffic have united members of both the House of Representatives and the Senate. Senate Judiciary Committee Chairman Orrin Hatch (R-Utah) has publicly rebuked the FBI's program, seconded by Senator Patrick Leahy (D-Vermont), usually Hatch's political opposite.

Last September, Representative Charles Canady (R-Florida) presented a bill to the House Judiciary Committee demanding severe restrictions on Carnivore. The bill passed with a 20-1 vote.
