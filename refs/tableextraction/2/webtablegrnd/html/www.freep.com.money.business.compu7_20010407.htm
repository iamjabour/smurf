Compuware stock price falls on revenue warning

April 7, 2001

BY JEFF BENNETT

FREE PRESS BUSINESS WRITER

Compuware Corp. shares fell as much as 18 percent in after-hours trading Friday after the software and computer services provider warned that it will hit earnings projections but miss analysts' revenue expectations.

Compuware shares fell to as low as $8 in trading after 4 p.m. the market closed. They fell 13 cents to $9.72 in regular trading.

The Farmington Hills-based company estimates its fourth-quarter revenues will be in the range of $505 million to $515 million, compared with the consensus analyst estimate of $548 million.

Final results for the quarter and Compuware's 2001 fiscal year will be announced May 2.

On July 21, 1999, Compuware had reported record first-quarter earnings of $90.7 million but the following day, the company's stock fell more than 20 percent to $26.75, when analysts saw that Compuware's software licensing revenues were $22 million below expectations.

Analysts look for high-tech companies to produce strong revenues and grow rapidly. This time, they were expecting Compuware to post a 10.6-percent revenue growth rate over its last quarter.

If Compuware hits the $515 million expectation, the growth rate will be 3.9 percent.

The revenues will still be significantly below Compuware's fourth-quarter results a year ago when it posted revenues of $582.1 million.

Joe Nathan, Compuware president and chief operating officer, attributed a $10-million to $15-million fall in revenue to changes in the foreign currency market and $15-million to $20-million in reorganization of the professional services business.

"Foreign currency exchange rates significantly dampened what would have been a solid quarter, given current economic conditions," Nathan said. "But we are pleased with the sequential growth we see in our earnings and distributed product line, evidence that our transition to a transaction-based business model is succeeding."

Nathan said in the professional services division, the company is cutting out workers and small pieces of the business that have become obsolete because of technology or don't fit the core business. He did not say how many jobs have been eliminated.

The division has been a problem for Compuware since the end of the fears of 2000 conversion problems when it was left with a high number of information technology workers who were not assigned to money-generating products. Compuware had hoped to reassign those workers to Internet-related projects, but the Internet work has not reached the level anticipated.

Earnings per share -- before amortization expense -- are estimated to be in a range of 15 cents to 17 cents, which is on target with the consensus analyst estimate of 16 cents.
