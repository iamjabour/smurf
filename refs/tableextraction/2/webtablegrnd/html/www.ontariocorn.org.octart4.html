CORN RESEARCH

Value-Enhanced Corn

By Ken Hough, Director, Research and Market Development, OCPA

Value-Enhanced Corn in the United States

In its recently released "1997-1998 Value-Enhanced Corn Quality Report", the U.S. Feed Grains Council provides an extensive analysis of current and anticipated future market trends for value-enhanced corn (VEC), i.e. corn with particular quality characteristics that add end-user value. VEC includes traditional specialty corn crops, such as white, waxy, hard endosperm/food grade, high amylose or high lysine corn, and more recently high oil corn and other nutritionally dense hybrids (enhanced protein and/or amino acid levels). The high oil and nutritionally dense corns currently represent the most significant growth potential for the VEC market since these are targeted livestock feed. White and waxy were also cited as having modest growth potential. (See Table 1).

Of 890 producers surveyed, the portion of growers producing VEC increased from 5.6 per cent in 1995 to 10.5 per cent in 1997, with 13 per cent projected for 1998. However, a high turnover rate in producers growing VEC was noted (16 per cent who grew VEC in ‘97 were not intending to in ‘98, but 33 per cent of the growers planning to produce VEC in ‘98 did not in ‘97). Factors attracting growers were premiums and previous experience with VEC, whereas the lack of (adequate) incentives and contentment with current hybrids/returns were cited as deterrents to producers considering VEC production.

High oil corn is the most commonly grown VEC (4 per cent of all producer survey respondents), followed by waxy and white corn (each at 2 per cent of respondents). Currently, about 40-50 per cent of the U.S. VEC is produced under contract – including all of the high amylose, organic, and high starch corn, and 46 per cent of the white, 54 per cent of the waxy, 43 per cent of the high oil and 47 per cent of the hard endosperm types. Interestingly, the number of elevators planning to make changes to handle segregated (identity-preserved) grain lots increased from 30 per cent in 1996 to 40 per cent in 1997.

Hard endosperm and white corns exhibited the highest starch contents (71.3-71.6 per cent, compared to 70.5 per cent for commodity corn), and the highest vitreous (hard) endosperm levels (87 and 89 per cent respectively, compared to 80-81 per cent for commodity corn), enhancing the grit yields and/or snack food processing characteristics of these corn types. High amylose corn had 62.3 per cent starch content, and only 36.7 percent wet-milling starch yield, compared to 64.3-66.2 per cent wet-milling starch yield for hard-endosperm or low-temperature dried corn, and 65.1 per cent starch yield for elevator and export samples.

Average oil content of high oil hybrids was 7.2 per cent in 1997, compared to 4.4 per cent for commodity corn. Fibre content was also very high in high oil corn (2.9 per cent) as well as in high amylose corn (3.1 per cent), compared to 2.3-2.5 per cent overall, and 2.2 per cent for white corn. Fibre content is related to increased germ size and small kernel size.

White and hard endosperm/food grade types exhibited the highest test weights (61.1-61.7 lb/bu in 1997, compared to 57.0 for commodity corn). High amylose had the lowest test weight (51.3). Overall, highest true density corresponded to the highest test weight values, verifying that test weight is a reasonable proxy for density, an important trait in food grade production.

Growth of Value-Enhanced Corn in Ontario

OCPA would concur with these U.S. market trend predictions as being generally applicable for Ontario. However, we believe there is high potential for expanding hard endosperm/food grade corn production in Ontario over the next decade. Also, 1998 is the first year Ontario producers and feed manufacturers have evaluated high oil corn first hand. Nevertheless, if yield potential and adaptability of high oil hybrids is demonstrated for Ontario, the livestock sector (hogs, poultry and cattle) should be a significant beneficiary. In the U. S., on average, high oil and conventional corn yields were comparable, but there was considerable range in results among producers. Generally, high oil hybrids will be out-yielded by conventional hybrids under ideal growing conditions, but yields have been more comparable under less than ideal conditions.

There may also be opportunity to expand Ontario’s waxy maize production in the near term to supply domestic processing – Nacan in Collingwood currently sources most of approximately 1.4 million bu. of waxy corn from the U.S. – if suitable hybrids, production contracts and premiums (currently about 55 cents/bu), and supply brokerage arrangements are available.

Food Grade Corn Market Expansion in Ontario

Significant opportunities – estimated at about $56 million annually – exist over the longer term for Ontario corn in food grade processing as illustrated in Table 2, based on Brian Doidge’s 1997 report "Food Grade Corn: Expanding the Industry in Ontario." Yellow food grade markets include expansion of dry milling operations, such as King Milling in Chatham, to supply the flaking grit market at Kellogg’s in London, Ontario and, potentially Battle Creek, Michigan. Southwestern Ontario, with its proven track record for production of high quality dry-milling corn, is geographically closer to Battle Creek than Kellogg’s traditional source of supply in central Indiana and Illinois.

A ready market for yellow and white food grade corn exists with corn snack food manufacturers, such as Hostess Frito/Lay and others with plants nearby in southwestern and central Ontario. Hostess Frito/Lay, in addition to its Cambridge manufacturing facility, has plants in Quebec, Nova Scotia, Detroit and well over a dozen other sites in the Northeastern U.S., all with excellent transportation links with southwestern Ontario and closer to us than to the Midwestern U.S.

Our challenge is to develop hybrids adapted to Ontario maturity with suitable food grade quality characteristics – without sacrificing agronomic performance – and to establish a reputation for being able to consistently supply the quality and quantity of grain that food grade processors demand. This is the motivation for the "Food Grade Quality and Grain Drying/Storage" research programs spearheaded by OCPA, with active participation of seed corn companies, corn processors, University of Guelph (at Ridgetown and Guelph) and Agriculture and Agri-Food Canada at Ottawa. The Grow Ontario program grant has been greatly appreciated through 1997 and 1998.
