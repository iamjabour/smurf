Circulation in the Mediterranean Outflow from neutrally buoyant float data

Michael D. Sparrow (&Vassilis Zervakis, Walter Zenk, Alán Cantos-Figuerola and John Gould...)

Abstract - RAFOS float data from the Eurofloat experiment have been examined in conjunction with historical float data in order to deduce oceanic motions and calculate Lagrangian statistics in the north-east Atlantic at the depth of the Mediterranean Water (MW) tongue. In the Canary Basin selected SOFAR data from the Mediterranean Outflow Experiment (MOE) have been analysed with the Eurofloat data. In this region, floats exhibit two distinct types of motion: Low frequency "zonal" motions within the core of the tongue and "wave-like" oscillations to the south. There is no simple classical westward advection at the depth of the MW tongue, even when floats caught within Meddies are excluded from the analysis. At this depth the most easterly branch of the Azores Current (AC) has an appreciable influence on the flow field. To the north of the Canary Basin RAFOS float data from the Institute f ò r Meereskunde launched at the MW level (SFB133) are analysed. Much of this data includes floats launched in Meddies. Eulerian velocity means, calculated from floats within Meddies and those outside of Meddies, suggest very different mean motions. Analysis of the "non-Meddy" data produce a mean flow to the north-west of up to 2.7 ± 0.4 cm s -1 , whereas in general the "Meddy" data give mean velocities to the south-west with associated velocities as high as 6.8 ± 3.4 cm s -1 . This demonstrates that Meddies do not move passively with the mean flow at 1000 m.

The zonal components of the integral time scales (T), length scales (L) and diffusivities (K) are larger than the meridional in both the "zonal" and "wave-like" float groups in the Canary Basin. The "zonal" floats have higher values of T, L and K and are more isotropic than the "wave-like" floats. R.m.s velocities of both the "Meddy" and "non-Meddy" SFB133 data are larger and more isotropic than the Eurofloat/MOE group. For the Eurofloat/MOE data peak Eddy Kinetic Energies (EKE's) are observed in the 60-120 day period. The "zonal" floats peak at about 12-13 cm 2 s -1 for both the zonal and meridional components whereas the "wave-like" floats have a less isotropic energy distribution with the zonal component being about half (7 cm 2 s -1 ) the meridional. In general, EKE's are higher in the SFB133 float groups than the Eurofloat/MOE group to the south. For the "non-Meddy" floats energies peak at 50 cm 2 s -1 in the 60-120 day band. However, the largest peak occurs at a period of about 10 days, with energies of about 120 cm 2 s -1 . The Lagrangian spectra of the "Meddy" floats have major peaks at periods of 2-5 days and 5-10 days, with associated energies of up to 1100 and 400 cm 2 s -1 , respectively. In general spectral slopes are between -2 and -3 for time periods greater than about 10 days, agreeing with the results of previous authors.

  1. Introduction

The Eurofloat experiment is part of an international effort funded by the European Commission MAST II programme to investigate the circulation of deep (1000-1750 m) water masses (Labrador Sea Water (LSW) and Mediterranean Water (MW)) in the eastern North Atlantic Ocean. The project involves Lagrangian studies using neutrally buoyant RAFOS and Marvor floats complemented by modelling work. Twenty one Marvor floats were launched in Sept. ’97 between 40 and 55 ° N and 15 and 25 ° W at depths of approximately 1750 m to study the circulation of LSW (Speer et al., 1998).

This paper is mainly concerned with the data from thirteen RAFOS floats deployed at the MW level ( » 1000 m) during the Poseidon 200/2 cruise in September ‘95 (Figure 1a). The data are examined and discussed in relation to the spreading of MW and the circulation of the north-east Atlantic. Eddy Kinetic Energy (EKE) and Lagrangian statistics based on the original work of Taylor (1929) are calculated and contrasted with the results of previous authors. In order to increase the data coverage of the region and the significance of the Lagrangian statistics, the data are supplemented with historical observations at the depth of the MW.

When comparing Lagrangian statistics it is important to have data gathered at similar depths to try to avoid any possible depth bias of the statistics (B Ø ning, 1988). In the Canary Basin region, selected data from the Mediterranean Outflow Experiment (MOE) (e.g. Spall et al., 1993; Richardson et al., 1989; Armi et al., 1989) have been analysed in conjunction with the Eurofloat data (Figure 2). In order to have data coverage in the dynamically important region to the north of the Eurofloat and MOE data (and to the south of the Eurofloat Marvor floats), RAFOS float data from the Institut f ò r Meereskunde (IfM) launched at the MW level are analysed (Figure 3). This work was funded by Deutsche Forschungsgemeinschaft (DFG) in the frame of Sonderforschungsbereich 133 (referred to as SFB 133). Many of the SFB133 floats were purposely launched in Meddies (K ¬ se and Zenk, 1996; Schultz Tokos et al., 1994; Schultz Tokos and Rossby, 1991). This has afforded us a unique opportunity to study the different behaviour of floats moving with the mean flow and those within Meddies at the depth of the MW and to contrast with the region to the south containing the Eurofloat/MOE floats.

The main frontal features in the region of interest are the Azores Front (AF), the Cape Verde Frontal Zone (CVFZ) and the Northwest African Upwelling Front (NWAUF). The latter is an intermittent seasonal feature, dependent on local wind forcing (e.g. Siedler and Onken, 1996) and is of minor concern to this study.

The Azores Current (AC), associated with the AF, is the southernmost of two branches of the Gulf Stream (the other being the North Atlantic Current) that bifurcates east of 50 ° W (Käse and Krauss, 1996). The AF extends zonaly along approximately 35 ° N, until about 17 ° W when the front is observed to turn sharply to the south (Käse and Siedler, 1982 ; Gould, 1985; Siedler et al.,1985 ; Käse et al.,1985). On the basis of a geostrophic analysis of historical hydrographic data, Klein and Siedler (1989) note that the southward motion of the AC appears to be organised into three bands; the first close to the eastern flank of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge (AC1), the second in the Central Canary Basin (AC2), and the third in the Canary Current close to the eastern boundary off Africa (AC3) (see Figure 4). The Azores Current forms large meanders as it crosses the Atlantic, with first order wavelengths around 600 km and second order wavelengths of about 200 km (Käse et al., 1985 ; Siedler and Onken., 1996). Typical maximum surface velocities of the jet are 20-40 cm s -1 with transports of 10-15 Sv (1Sv = 10 6 m 3 s -1 ) (Käse and Krauss, 1996).

The CVFZ is formed along the boundary between North and South Atlantic Central Waters (NACW and SACW, respectively) (Sverdrup et al., 1942 ; Tomczak, 1984 ; Emery and Meincke, 1986). This boundary extends zonaly along the Atlantic at about 15 ° N until 30 ° W when it takes on a more south-eastern orientation (Zenk et al., 1991).

The water masses of the North Atlantic are numerous and varied (e.g. Ríos et al., 1992 ; Spall et al., 1993 ; Arhan et al., 1994 ). The main water masses of interest in this study are MW (e.g. Käse et al., 1986), Subpolar Mode Water (SPMW) and LSW of high latitude origin (e.g. McCartney and Talley, 1982), NACW and SACW (e.g. Emery and Meincke, 1986) and Subantarctic Intermediate Water (SAAIW) (e.g. Käse et al., 1986).

MW traces its origin to the north western Mediterranean. It is characterised by anomalously high temperatures ( » 7-11 ° C) and salinities ( » 35.1-36.0) at an approximate depth of 1000 m (e.g. Worthington, 1976). The existence of the MW plume used to be interpreted as a result of a well balanced ratio of slow advection and turbulent diffusion from its source in the Gulf of Cadiz (e.g. Defant, 1956 ; Joseph and Sendner 1958 ; Richardson and Mooney, 1975). However, further analyses (e.g. Reid, 1978 ; Daniault, 1995 ; Käse and Zenk, 1996) reveal that the salt tongue does not simply spread radially from its source, but rather is limited by eastward advection of fresh water masses to the south from the South Atlantic, and to the north from the Labrador Sea. Geostrophic analyses do not show a smooth current field within the MW plume (e.g. Kase and Zenk, 1996). One of the main reasons for this is the presence of salt lenses (or Meddies) containing large amounts of original Gibraltar Overflow Water in their interior (e.g. McDowel and Rossby, 1978 ; Armi and Zenk, 1984 ; Käse et al., 1985 ; McWilliams 1985 ; Käse and Zenk, 1996). Meddies gradually, or spontaneously release high saline water into their surroundings, providing a new agent for salt transport. The motion of Meddies and their contribution to the salt tongue are still relatively poorly understood.

Over the last few years the use of neutrally buoyant floats has become an increasingly cost effective way to examine currents and water movements. Float trajectories allow us to examine both the large scale flow and eddy fields and give insight into particle drift. As well as improving our understanding of the general circulation and allowing an estimation of the influence of eddies on the mean flow via Lagrangian statistics and energy spectra these measurements are important in developing and checking model output (e.g. Schmitz and Holland, 1982 ; Davis et al., 1996).

2. Measurements and Data

The Eurofloat floats were deployed in the region between the Azores and the Canary Islands from the F/S Poseidon in Sept./Oct. 1995 at an approximate depth of 950 m in order to trace the circulation within the Mediterranean water tongue (Figure 1a). The quantity of data recorded by each float varied, partly because the floats were programmed for different mission lengths, but also due to the early surfacing of some floats thought to be caused by seawater leakage. This problem is thought to have been caused by the erosion of the float's stainless steel release plugs which will be replaced in future experiments.

The MOE was carried out between 1984 and 1988 and involved using SOFAR floats to investigate how advection and diffusion produced the westward extension of the salt tongue. Richardson et al. (1989) examined data from floats caught within Meddies. They speculated that Meddies move south-westward because their upper portion lies within the generally south-westward return flow of the subtropical gyre. Spall et al. (1993) examined data from the MOE that excluded Meddies. They found two dominant patterns of variability : Quasi-zonal motions (referred to as the "zonal" groups of floats), which they attributed to baroclinic instability of the large scale flow, but which K ¬ se and Zenk (1996) suggest is due to Meddy dynamics; and wave-like oscillations (the "wave-like" group of floats) postulated to be caused by radiating Rossby waves generated to the south in the CVFZ. For the present analysis only non Meddy MOE data east of 30ºW are considered and following Spall (per. com.) two of the floats (128 and 148) were selectively truncated to remove data that were in Meddies. Following the analysis of Spall et al. (1993) the data (Eurofloat and MOE) are split into "wave-like" and "zonal" groups (see Figures 1 and 2).

The SFB133 floats were launched to trace and study Meddy structures (K ¬ se and Zenk, 1996; Schultz Tokos et al., 1994; Schultz Tokos and Rossby, 1991), though a significant number were also launched outside of Meddies. In this analysis the SFB133 floats (Fig 3) are split into two groups - those thought to be in Meddies and those not. The gradation was achieved by examining the individual float tracks and in situ temperature from the float's temperature sensor (which have an estimated accuracy of ` 0.08 ° C (Boebel et al.,1995)). Floats seen to exhibit tight spirals are, for this analysis, considered to be of the "Meddy" group. Meddies are distinguished by their high salt and heat content, about 1 psu and 2.5ºC greater than their surrounding environment (e.g. Schultz Tokos and Rossby, 1991). The float's temperature trace can therefore sometimes be used to examine whether an individual float is captured or ejected by a Meddy. It should be pointed out that Richardson (1993), by examining historical float data within eddies, showed that the north-east Atlantic contained anticyclones of roughly the same size as Meddies, though with slower swirl speeds and warmer warm core anomalies. This analysis cannot easily distinguish between these two types of "Meddy-like" anticyclones and so all are treated as part of the "Meddy" group.

3. Circulation patterns

i) Eurofloat floats

General observations of the flow characteristics from the Eurofloat RAFOS floats (Figure 1a) can readily be made. Two floats (165 and 170) are captured by the Azores Current and travel in a generally eastward direction with a mean eastward velocity of 4.2 ` 1.0 cm s -1 . These floats pass to the north-east of Madeira and thus are in the most easterly branch of the Azores Current (AC3) (see Figure 4). The tracks of 165 and 170 are strongly influenced by the bottom topography, following closely the bathymetry between 3-4 kms. Although the AC is mostly confined the upper thermocline (< 800 m) (e.g. Stramma, 1984 ; Käse et al., 1986) this study shows it still has appreciable influence at the depths of the floats ( » 950 m).

As floats 165 and 170 are captured by AC3, floats 163, 164 and 166 are influenced by the branch of the AC (AC2) that moves southwards between the Cruiser ( » 32N, 29W) and Great Meteor ( » 30N, 29W) Seamounts, and Madeira ( » 33N, 17W) (see Figure 4). Float 166 moves westward before being drawn southward, probably as a direct result of AC2. Floats 163 and 164 initially move together - westwards and then southwards, where after about 100 days 164 surfaces. Float 163 describes a large anticyclonic loop, taking about 500 days to complete the revolution. The float rounds the Cruiser Seamount at its furthest east, whilst exhibiting minor wave and looping behaviour throughout its journey. It may be that this represents some kind of gyre/eddy system between two branches (AC1 and AC2) of the Azores Current, though the permanency of such a mechanism cannot be investigated in this analysis.

The tracks of floats 161 and 162 are orientated in a northwest to southeast direction. Similar "wave-like" behaviour of floats in the same area are also observed in the SOFAR MOE float results of Spall et al. (1993, 1996). This motion is probably due to the effect of radiating Rossby waves generated to the south in the Cape Verdiere Frontal Zone (Spall et al., 1993, 1996). As the floats of Spall et al. (1993,1996) were active during 1984-88 and the Eurofloat floats missions lasted from 1995-97 this would therefore seem to be a permanent, or at least recurring feature of the circulation in the Canary Basin.

Two of the floats (167 and 169), to the north-west of the approximate position of the maximum of the Mediterranean salt tongue, move in anti-cyclonic arcs, in a north-west direction. Float 169 shows a fairly average mean temperature of 10.5 ° C. However, the temperature of the surrounding water of float 167 suddenly drops by about 0.2 ° C at approximately 22.5 ° W with little change in depth (Figure 6) whilst the float turns strongly northward. This could be the signal of the float moving into slightly cooler and fresher Azores re-circulated water (Cromwell et al.,1995). The northward component of motion of floats 167 and 169 (with an average northward velocity of 1.7 ± 1.3cm s -1 ) north of about 34.6 ° N, is in the opposite direction to the surface winds. This agrees with the observations of Saunders (1982), Maillard (1986) and the results of Spall et al. (1993) who predicted a northward motion of 0.5 cm s -1 at 1100 m.

Float 121 initially moves eastward, towards the Gulf of Cadiz, exhibiting minor looping behaviour as it does so. At about 12 ° W the float becomes influenced by an anti-cyclonic eddy like feature, eventually looping the feature twice before moving again east possibly becoming affected by the NWAUF. The temperature history along the float's path shows an initial average temperature of about 10.9 ° C, followed by a gradual drop to 10.2 ° C, between days 50 to 60. During the time the float encounters the eddy feature the temperature of the surrounding water drops by about 0.25 ° C. These temperature characteristics suggest that the float, though influenced by a Meddy, did not penetrate the its core characteristics. Float 160 moves westward, down the mean salinity gradient of the MW tongue, with an average westward velocity of 2.0 ± 1.8 cm s -1 . It starts to loop cyclonically shortly before surfacing. Float 157 remains to the north of the Canary Islands, exhibiting a seemingly random walk behaviour.

Unfortunately float 125 suffered substantial data loss (Figures 1a and 1b). At the start of the mission the float was exhibiting the strong looping behaviour characteristic of a Meddy, with a period of 4 days. During this time the float's temperature sensor showed 10.4-11.0 ° C. The next set of good data, just to the west of a seamount at approximately 30N,16W shows the float was still in the Meddy, though the period had increased to 10-12 days and the temperature range decreased to 9.0-9.5 ° C with no observable change in depth. The float finally surfaces to the west with little change in latitude. The almost purely westward motion is interesting, as usually floats trapped in eddies move to the south-west. It is probable that the float remained in the eddy throughout its life as there are no remarkable changes in temperature during the float's lifetime to suggest otherwise.

ii) General circulation

Mean Eulerian velocities may be calculated from the floats by averaging all the velocity data within a certain area or 'box'. Using a smaller box size gives more resolution, but increases the error of the mean calculated (see Section 5 for a more detailed description). The area covered by the float data are divided into grid boxes, and means and associated errors calculated. Different box sizes were tried and a 4º by 4º grid found to give a suitable balance between resolution and error in the means. The MOE/Eurofloat (Figure 5), "Non-Meddy" and "Meddy" SFB133 (Figure 6) float groups are processed separately. Only mean values that exceeded their associated error are presented.

The means calculated from the MOE/Eurofloat data set are, bar two exceptions, less than about 1 cm s -1 . These exceptions occur at 36ºN, 28ºW, producing a mean flow of 3.6 ± 1.1 cm s -1 to the north-west and at 36ºN,12ºW, with an associated mean of 3.0 ± 1.4 cm s -1 to the east. Those means of Figure 6 in a darker shade seem to be influenced by branches of the AC (compare with Figure 4). Interestingly, average inflow into the Mediterranean is suggested near the Gulf of Gibralter, though the associated errors are relatively large. It is clear that there is no simple average westward advection at the depth of the MW tongue.

The means calculated from the "Meddy" and "Non-Meddy" groups of the SFB133 data (Figure 7) suggest very different motions for floats (or water particles) caught within rather than outside of Meddies. The "Non-Meddy" data shows a mean flow to the north-west of up to 2.7 ± 0.4 cm s -1 , whereas the "Meddy" data give mean velocities to the south-west (except at 40ºN, 12ºW where the flow is to the south-east, but the error associated with the mean here is large) with velocities as high as 6.8 ± 3.4 cm s -1 . This implies that, on average, a particle caught within an a Meddy will be advected in a different direction to one that is not. This demonstrates that Meddies do not move passively with the mean flow at 1000 m. This may be because there is some connection with the surface flow (as suggested by Richardson et al., 1989) or because the Meddies are forming a vortex pair, giving rise to a Meddy induced advection rate.

Although useful information can be gleaned from examining mean velocities and the tracks of individual floats, this is limited in quantifying the influence of eddies and rings on the large scale flow. The next section attempts to address this problem.

4. Lagrangian Statistics

The effect of eddies and rings on advection and mixing processes in the ocean are of fundamental importance. They influence both passive and non passive tracers and are the most sensitive statistical method of testing the validity of eddy resolving models (e.g. Krauss and B Ø ning, 1987). Lagrangian statistics provide the most direct description of property transport (Davis, 1983).

Arriving to statistically significant conclusions from Lagrangian measurements requires the separation of the flow into mean and perturbation components. To obtain the mean velocity U, V (zonal, meridional components respectively) of the flow, the ensemble (time and space) average of the velocities of all the floats within a region of spatial scale l must be defined. Minimising l gives more resolution, but increases the error d U of the mean velocity estimate, since the product d U ×l is a constant dependent on the sampling density and eddy field properties (Davis, 1991).

i) Results from the Eurofloat and MOE data

The first group (group (1) from Table 1) is the combined Eurofloat and MOE data. Only data from the MOE outside of Meddies and east of 30ºW are used in this analysis. Data from Eurofloat RAFOS 125 are excluded because of its poor data quality and because the float spent most of its mission in a Meddy (see Section 3). The approach taken to separate the mean and perturbation velocities varies between authors. The mean velocity of each float could be used to represent the regional mean. However, the mean velocities of individual floats can vary quite considerably and therefore the usual practice is to use means based on several floats either by considering a 'box' of certain area and calculating the mean of all floats within this box (e.g. Krauss and B Ø ning, 1987) or, for example, using latitudinal means (e.g. Spall et al., 1993). We adopt the former and consider several boxes containing the float data (Figure 8).

In relating particle transport to statistical quantities of the variability, Taylor (1921) made the assumption that the time dependent motion was represented by homogeneous stationary turbulence. Analysing floats within strong mean current shear causes overestimates of T, L and K (see appendix of Krauss and B Ø ning (1987)). In the Eurofloat/MOE area the strongest source of mean (mostly zonal) current shear is the AC. It is subjective deciding which floats are 'significantly' affected by the mean shear of the AC. We have analysed the data without the two floats (165 and 170) thought to have been captured by the Azores Current as prior calculations with only the Eurofloat data demonstrated that including floats 165 and 170 increased the estimate of T11 by almost 90% for an associated decrease in T22 of approximately 10% (with estimated errors of around 5%). This demonstrates the importance of removing floats caught within current shear if Taylor (1921) based Lagrangian statistics are to be calculated.

Except for the zonal component of the "zonal" floats the mean flow is not statistically different from zero, whilst the perturbation flow is significantly stronger. The "zonal" floats show a significant mean westward advection of 0.77 ± 0.44 cm s -1 . As there is minimal Meddy influence in this data set, this confirms that within the area covered by the Eurofloat/MOE floats (see Table 1, Figures 1 and 2) there is a mean westward advection within the salt tongue. The zonal components of T, L and K are larger than the meridional in both the "zonal" and "wave-like" float groups. These differences are thought to be due to both current shear (e.g. Krauss and Böning, 1987) and the b effect (Rhines, 1976). The differences that remain between the meridional and zonal components of T, L and K are probably mainly due to the b effect, though it should be noted that some meridional shear could still remain. The "zonal" floats have higher values of T, L and K than the "wave-like" floats, agreeing with the results of Spall et al.(1993) that the "zonal" floats are more effective at mixing than the "wave-like" floats which are periodic in nature. The "wave-like" floats also more isotropic than either the "zonal" or previous authors' results such as groups (1) and (2) from Table 2. The "zonal" values of T are slightly higher than those in the western North Atlantic (groups 1 and 2 from Table 2) whereas the values of L are a little lower. This might be expected because of the higher eddy activity and hence r.m.s perturbation velocities observed in the western North Atlantic (see equation 7). The values of K from this analysis are lower than those in the western North Atlantic from groups 1 and 2, but close to the values of Spall et al (1993 ) (group 3 from Table 2) and the 1.5-3 x 10 7 cm 2 s -1 estimated by Needler and Heath (1975).

The weighted means (Table 4) of the "non-Meddy" SFB133 data show a statistically significant mean northward and westward velocity (though it should be noted that the uncertainties are large). The northward mean flow is stronger because of floats 16 and 29 from group (2) which show a strong northward motion (see Figure 3). Without these two floats there is still an average northward motion (see for example group (1)), though reduced. The Meddy floats are advected with a statistically significant westward and southward motion, as suggested by the analysis of mean velocities in Section 3. The fact that the Meddy motion is to the southwest, but the flow outside of eddies is to the north(west) is important as it implies that a particle caught within an a Meddy will be, on average advected in a different direction to one that is not. The average velocity of a float caught within an Meddy would be 1.5 cm s -1 , making an angle of approximately 230º from the north, where as the average velocity of a float outside of a Meddy would be 1.3 cm s -1 at 335º from the north. This compares to an average of 0.74 ± 0.54 cm s -1 at 210º for three Meddies and 2.6 ± 0.6 cm s -1 at 253º for three anti-cyclones observed in the Eastern Basin (Richardson, 1993). This demonstrates that Meddies do not move passively with the mean flow at 1000 m. This may be because there is some connection with the surface flow (Richardson et al., 1989) or that the Meddies are forming vortex pairs (K ¬ se and Zenk, 1996), giving rise to a Meddy induced advection rate.

R.m.s velocities in both the "Meddy" and "non-Meddy" groups are isotropic and higher than the Eurofloat/MOE group, almost double in the eddy groups. This is probably due to the higher eddy activity and Eddy Kinetic Energies (EKE's) (see Section 5) of the more northern SFB133 data set which are closer to the formation region of Meddies off Cape St. Vincent. A float or water particle caught within an Meddy, or in a region of high Meddy activity, will experience a fast r.m.s velocity.

The Integral Time and Length scales of the "non-Meddy" group are smaller and more isotropic than the "zonal" Eurofloat/MOE group or groups (1) and (2) (of Table 2) in the western North Atlantic. The T values of less than 4 days reflect the high EKE of the region (even after the removal of the Meddy floats). The diffusivities of the "non-Meddy" floats are higher than those of the "wave-like" or the meridional component of the "zonal" floats, but lower than the zonal component of the "zonal" floats.

5. Lagrangian Energy Spectra

The Lagrangian Energy Spectra are estimated for the different float groups (see Table 1) by dividing each float time series into separate pieces, computing the spectra and averaging over the number of pieces. In order to examine different details of the energy spectra the spectra are calculated for different length pieces, ranging from 30-300 days. The figures presented here show the results from using the longest possible piece lengths so as to resolve the greatest number of frequencies.

i) Eurofloat and MOE floats

Using data from the MOE, Spall et al.(1993) examined the Lagrangian Energy Spectra, binning the data into three frequency bands representing time periods greater than 200 days, 50-200 days and less than 50 days. We consider the MOE data west of 30ºW and also the additional Eurofloat data (see Section 2). Both the zonal and meridional components of the "zonal" floats peak at 13 cm 2 s -2 at about 60-120 days (Figure 9a). For time periods greater than about 120 days the "zonal" floats have a greater zonal component than meridional. Between about 100 and 10 days the meridional component is the greater, and less than 10 days both components are fairly isotropic. This follows the broad trend found by Spall et al.(1993), though with greater peak energies. In comparison, the "wave-like" floats (Figure 10a) show a greater meridional than zonal component for all resolvable periods, the greatest difference occurring at about 60-120 days when, like the "zonal" floats, the zonal and meridional components peak at about 7 and 12 cm 2 s -2 , respectively. As pointed out in Spall et al. (1993), the increased meridional component is probably a result of the increased north-south orientation of the oscillations in most of the wave floats. The surface drifters of Krauss and B Ø ning (1987) show an energy concentration between 20 and 50 days for their Ensemble 1 in the same general area as the Eurofloat/MOE data set. This may correspond to a secondary peaks marked on Figure 9a, but is swamped by the maxima at about 60-120 days. Their peak energies were higher (40 cm 2 s -2 ), as would be expected for drifters at the surface and their energy spectra also show a greater meridional than zonal component between 20 and 50 days, in agreement with the "zonal" floats of this analysis.

Assigning a distinct slope to the slope of the spectra is notoriously difficult. Krauss and B Ø ning (1987) found the spectra fell off predominantly with -2, but in certain cases the slope was better described by a -3 gradient. Colin de Verdiere (1983) obtained a well defined -2 slope for his surface data, whereas Freeland et al. (1975) obtained slopes like -4 at 1500 m depth. The "zonal" spectra (Figure 9b) show slopes close to -2 and the "wave-like" (Figure 10b) between -2 and -3, for periods greater than about 10 days. For periods less than 10 days the spectral slopes have an associated gradient between -1 and -2.

ii) SFB133 floats :

The SFB133 floats are divided into two groups, those that are deemed to be in Meddies ("Meddy" groups) and those not ("non-Meddy" groups) (see Section 2). Some of the SFB133 floats had three position fixes per day, others only one. Rather than lose possible detail of the spectra by averaging all the data to one position fix per day the two "Meddy" and "non-Meddy" groups are further subdivided into those with three position fixes and those with one.

Looking first at the "non-Meddy" groups, the floats with one position fix per day (and the longest time series) (Figure 11a) show peaks around 50 cm 2 s -2 at about 70-90 days for the meridional and 30-50 for the zonal components. The meridional peak is equivalent to the 60-120 day peak observed in the "zonal" float group to the south. It is difficult to tell whether the displacement of the zonal peak is a real feature or an artefact of the uncertainties. The 3-daily non-Meddy data (Figure 12a) show higher energy at higher frequencies than the daily floats, though the time series are too short to examine the spectra at time periods greater than 60 days. At the longest time periods resolvable, there is a peak in energies of about 40 cm 2 s -2 for the zonal and 25 cm 2 s -2 for the meridional at approximately 20 days. This is due to inertial currents which have a time period of around 20 days at these latitudes. The largest peak of 120 cm 2 s -2 occurs at 10 days ( what could be the cause of this ?) . Several peaks representing tidal currents occur with periods between about 0.5 and 3 days, with the largest of these peaks reaching energies of 90 cm 2 s -2 . There is a noticeable bias of zonal energies over meridional.

The daily and 3-daily "Meddy" floats show isotropic energy spectra at higher frequencies, as might be expected from floats trapped within circular vortices. Both spectra show two main energy peaks (Figures 13a and 14a). The first, between 5 and 10 days peaks at 400 cm 2 s -2 for the daily and 280 cm 2 s -2 for the 3-daily floats. The second occurs between 2 and 5 days with peak energies of 1100 cm 2 s -2 and 850 cm 2 s -2 for the daily and 3-daily Meddy floats, respectively. A third peak is observable in the daily floats at about 20 days, peaking at about 70 cm 2 s -2 in the zonal and just over 50 cm 2 s -2 in the meridional directions, corresponding to inertial currents. The peak energies in the SFB133 groups are higher than in the Eurofloat/MOE data set. This follows a broad trend of an increase of energies with increasing latitude as well as a reduction in the period of the strongest energy band seen in both Lagrangian (e.g. Krauss and B Ø ning, 1987) and Eulerian (e.g. Dickson, 1983) measurements.

The spectral slope of the daily non-Meddy group (Figure 11b) show a frequency plateau extending up to the frequency of the strong energy peak (see Figure 11a). After this cut-off frequency the energy drops off with a slope close to -2 until about 4 days. There is then a small plateau after which the slope falls off rapidly. The 3-daily "non-Meddy" group show a similar pattern. A well defined frequency plateau is not visible as the data are limited to 60 days. The slope again follows a -2 slope until about 4 days, after which a second plateau extends to a time period of about a day. For time periods less than a day the slope falls off rapidly. It is difficult to ascertain a slope to the daily "Meddy" group (Figure 13b) because of the presence of the two very strong energy peaks, giving rise to a plateau followed by a rapid fall of the slope. It is also difficult to assign a slope from the thrice daily group, though the gradient can be seen to increase with higher frequencies.

6. Conclusions

There seems to be no simple westward advection at the depth of the MW tongue, even when floats caught within Meddies are excluded from the analysis. In some areas the most easterly branch of the AC is observed to dominate the mean flow. Although the AC is mostly confined the upper thermocline (< 800 m) (e.g. Stramma, 1984 ; Käse et al., 1986) this study shows it still has appreciable influence at the depths of the floats ( » 950 m). The means calculated from floats within Meddies and those outside of Meddies suggest very different motions. The "Non-Meddy" data show a mean flow to the north-west of up to 2.7 ± 0.4 cm s -1 , whereas, in general, the "Meddy" data give mean velocities to the south-west with velocities as high as 6.8 ± 3.4 cm s -1 . This implies that a particle caught within a Meddy is advected in a different mean direction to one that is not, demonstrating that Meddies do not move passively with the mean flow at 1000 m. This may be because there is some connection with the surface flow (as suggested by Richardson et al., 1989) or because the Meddies are forming vortex pairs, giving rise to a Meddy induced advection rate.

Confirming the findings of Spall et al (1993), at the depth of the MW in the Eastern Basin region, floats exhibit two distinct types of motion; low frequency "zonal" motions within the core of the tongue and "wave-like" oscillations to the south. Lagrangian statistics provide the most direct description of property transport (Davis, 1983). Within the Eurofloat/MOE data the zonal component of the "zonal" floats show a significant mean westward velocity of 0.77 ± 0.44 cm s -1 , whereas in the rest of the data the mean flow is not statistically different from zero, whilst the perturbation flow is significantly stronger. The zonal components of T, L and K are larger than the meridional in both the "zonal" and "wave-like" float groups. These differences are thought to be due to both current shear (e.g. Krauss and Böning, 1987) and the b effect (Rhines, 1976). The "zonal" floats have higher values of T, L and K than the "wave-like" floats, agreeing with the results of Spall et al.(1993) that these are more effective at mixing than the "wave-like" floats which are periodic in nature. The "wave-like" floats also more isotropic than either the "zonal" floats or the results of previous work at the same depth in the western North Atlantic (Riser and Rossby, 1983). The "zonal" values of T are slightly higher than those in the western North Atlantic (Riser and Rossby, 1983) whereas the value of L are a little lower. This might be expected because of the higher eddy activity and hence r.m.s perturbation velocities observed in the western North Atlantic. The values of K from this analysis are lower than those in the western North Atlantic, but close to the values of Spall et al (1993 ) and the 1.5-3 x 10 7 cm 2 s -1 estimated by Needler and Heath (1975) in the eastern North Atlantic.

R.m.s velocities in both the "Meddy" and "non-Meddy" groups of the SFB133 data are more isotropic and higher than the Eurofloat/MOE group, almost double in the "Meddy" group. This is probably due to the higher eddy activity and EKE's of the more northern SFB133 data set which are closer to the formation region of Meddies off Cape St. Vincent. Obviously a float or water particle caught within an Meddy, or in a region of high Meddy activity, will experience a fast r.m.s velocity. T and L from the "non-Meddy" group are smaller and more isotropic than the "zonal" Eurofloat/MOE group or previous results in the western North Atlantic.

For the Eurofloat/MOE data peak EKE's are observed in the 60-120 day period. The "zonal" floats peak at about 12-13 cm 2 s -1 for both the zonal and meridional components whereas the "wave-like" floats have a less isotropic energy distribution with the zonal component being about half (7 cm 2 s -1 ) the meridional. As pointed out in Spall et al. (1993), the larger meridional component is probably a result of the increased north-south orientation of the oscillations in most of the "wave-like" floats. Spectral slopes of approximately -2 for the "zonal" group and between -2 and -3 for the "wave-like" floats are observed for periods greater than about 10 days. This is close to the results of Krauss and B Ø ning (1987) who found the spectra fell off predominantly with -2 (but also in certain cases by -3) and Colin de Verdiere (1983) who obtained a well defined -2 slope for his surface data. For periods less than 10 days the spectral slopes have a gradient between -1 to -2.

In general, EKE's are higher in the SFB133 float groups than the Eurofloat/MOE group to the south. This follows a broad trend of an increase of energies with increasing latitude as well as a reduction in the period of the strongest energy band seen in both Lagrangian (e.g. Krauss and B Ø ning, 1987) and Eulerian (e.g. Dickson, 1983) measurements. For the "non-Meddy" floats, energies peak at 50 cm 2 s -1 in the 60-120 day band. However, the largest peak occurs at a period of about 10 days, with energies of about 120 cm 2 s -1 . The Lagrangian spectra of the "Meddy" floats have major peaks at periods of 2-5 days and 5-10 days, with associated energies of up to 1100 and 400 cm 2 s -1 , respectively. The spectral slopes of the "Meddy" floats are difficult to ascertain , but seem to follow a -2 slope, but with intermittent plateaus where the slope is close to 0. For periods less than about a day the slopes fall off rapidly.

This study reinforces the notion that use of subsurface floats is a unique way to obtain a better understanding of large and mesoscale oceanic motions. The data obtained from the Eurofloat RAFOS floats cover areas of the North Atlantic for which information is relatively scarce.
