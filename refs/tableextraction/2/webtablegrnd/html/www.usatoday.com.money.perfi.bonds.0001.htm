Bonds: There's good news, bad news

Yields are the highest in years, but a flourishing bond market could mean a slower economy

By John Waggoner and Sandra Block, USA TODAY

Ups and downs of bonds, their prices

John Waggoner's Investing column: Money market funds may be better choice

Bond funds had a great third quarter, and they look good for next year, too. But don't start celebrating yet. The bond market is only happy when it rains.

The average U.S. government bond fund, among the most popular, rose 2.7% from June through September, and 6.8% for 2000. In the bond world, that's good: U.S. government bonds have risen an average 5.5% the past 60 years.

You should always be a bit worried when the bond market is happy. Bond prices typically rally when the economy is headed into a slowdown.

That's just what John Bender, co-manager of Strong Corporate bond fund, believes. He thinks U.S. gross domestic product growth slowed to about 3% in the third quarter, down from a torrid 5.6% in the second quarter.

Why? In large part because the Federal Reserve has raised short-term interest rates. That makes it more expensive for businesses to borrow.

Last year, consumers were flush with stock profits and spending enthusiastically. That's cooling, too. "We've seen a decline in stock prices, so the wealth effect is starting to reverse," he says.

If the economy is slowing, then inflation will probably stay under control, and the Federal Reserve won't raise rates again this year. That's good news for the bond market, which views inflation the way Dracula views garlic.

The one danger is that the economy could heat up. If one party dominates Congress and the White House next year, for instance, it would give the new president ? either George W. Bush or Al Gore ? a good chance of enacting a massive tax cut. That would get the economy roaring again, and threaten the federal budget surplus, says Bill Gross, manager of Pimco Total Return: "It would mean the surplus has a good chance of turning to a deficit."

Types of bond funds

What makes many bond funds attractive right now are their interest payments ? quite a comfort when the rest of your portfolio is standing still .

Because interest rates have been moving higher, bond yields ? the interest payment divided by the bond's price ? are the highest in years. That could change if the Fed cuts rates or suggests it's no longer worried about inflation, says Malcolm Makin , a financial planner in Westerly, R.I. "I'm locking in yields that probably won't exist six months from now."

But don't get carried away and chase bond funds with the highest yields. Pay attention to total return, which measures the bond fund's price and yield. For the past 12-month period, for example, yields on high-yield bond funds topped 10%. But since Jan. 1, the average high-yield bond fund's total return has fallen 2.6%. That suggests the yield comes with a lot of risk.

If you'd like to add income to your portfolio, there are dozens of bonds and bond funds. The chart at the end show how some popular bond fund types are doing this year. Some popular fund types:

Municipal bonds . The average general municipal bond is yielding 6.3%, according to fund tracker Lipper. But that's only part of the story. Because interest payments from municipal bonds are exempt from federal taxes, actual returns may be much higher, depending on your tax bracket. And when the difference between yields on municipal bonds and comparable Treasury bonds is small, as it is now, even investors in the 28% tax bracket can benefit from muni bonds, says Eric Jacobson, bond analyst for Morningstar.

Municipal bonds aren't appropriate for your 401(k) or individual retirement account, which are already tax-deferred. You can find a calculator to help compare municipal with taxable bonds at www.investinginbonds.com .

Mortgage bonds . Funds that invest in bonds issued by Freddie Mac, Ginnie Mae and Fannie Mae are yielding up to 6.6% now, making them a good deal for risk-averse investors, says David MacEwen , senior vice president of fixed-income investing for American Century. The three government-sponsored agencies package home mortgages in pools and sell them to investors. Ginnie Mae securities are backed by the full faith and credit of the U.S. government. Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae don't have the federal guarantee but both have huge lines of credit from the U.S. Treasury, making default unlikely.

U.S. government securities . Yields on Treasury securities have been falling because the government has cut its borrowing. The shrinking supply has driven up prices and lowered yields.

If investing in bonds backed by the U.S. government is important to you, you may be willing to accept the lower yield. Another option: Invest in a general bond fund that buys a mix of government securities and mortgage bonds.

If you're worried about inflation, consider inflation-indexed Treasury securities. Unlike traditional bonds, which pay a fixed interest payment until maturity, payments are adjusted to reflect changes in the consumer price index. As a result, prices tend to be less volatile than for traditional bonds, says John Hollyer, co-manager of the Vanguard Inflation-Protected Securities fund.
