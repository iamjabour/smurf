Section 1. Background: Schedule C (Form 1040)

Profit or Loss from Business or Profession (Sole Proprietorship)

Schedule C, as many of you are probably already aware, is the main form for filing business income & expenses as an adjunct to form 1040. The Schedule C form provides a relatively straightforward means for reporting business-related income & expenses; however, what may be legitimately included within the income & expense lines of Schedule C may not, at least not at first, always seem so straightforward. That is the main reason for this guide.

Schedule C can be used, then, to report business income & expenses, whether the business is service-oriented, sales-oriented, or both. By definition, however, a business filing a Schedule C is not a separately incorporated entity {e.g., an LLC or a registered, incorporated company}. Rather, the business {and its income or losses} are considered part & parcel of an individual's personal income. Of course, as a rule, this also means that all liabilities related to the business are also assumed as personal liabilities, whether for debts & expenses, or as a party subject to legal liabilities & remedies.

The basic parts of Schedule C, each of which will be discussed in greater detail in the sections following, are presented line-by-line, in the order they appear on the form, and include the following sections:

  • A. Preliminary Information
  • B. Part I: Income
  • C. Part II: Expenses
  • D. Final Tally
  • E. Part III: Cost of Goods Sold
  • F. Part IV: Information on Your Vehicle , and
  • G. Part V: Other Expenses

Whether you file a Schedule C on your own, then, or even if you use a 'paid preparer', Schedule C.com 's "Small Business Guide to Schedule C" can help you with useful information ... for your own use to prepare your own Schedule C return, or in order to understand Schedule C better and thereby be able to provide your paid preparer with full & more accurate information.

Before we begin looking at Schedule C line-by-line, then, it will probably be helpful to look at some of the numbers surrounding Schedule C ... like the total number of Schedule C returns filed, how many Schedule C returns are audited, and what some of the results of Schedule C audits were.

A. Number of Schedule C Forms Filed: 1975-1999

It is common knowledge that the number of small businesses in the United States has been growing fairly steadily for the past quarter century. For this past tax year (1998), the IRS estimated that, of the more than 123 million individual income returns filed, roughly 17.5 million [14.33%] would include a Schedule C. This compares with roughly 7.5 million Schedule C returns [or 8.87% of all individual income returns] filed in 1975.

As can be seen from Table 1, below, while the number of Schedule C returns continued to grow throughout the period, as a percentage of all individual returns filed, Schedule C returns actually flattened out in 1996-1997. However, if the IRS estimates are correct, the upward trend for Schedule C returns as a percentage of all individual returns filed will again continue for 1998 & 1999 ... and, we suspect, for the foreseeable future.

B. Number of Schedule C Returns Audited: Federal Fiscal Year 1997

Like all IRS forms, Schedule C forms are subject to occasional 'audit'. In the language of the IRS, audits are often referred to as 'examinations'. The IRS generally tracks these examinations by the federal fiscal year, so there may not be a complete correlation between numbers of returns, Schedule C returns, etc. Nonetheless, looking at a given year can provide some index to how susceptible Schedule C returns are to being audited, as well as what the outcomes of those audits have been. {Of course, recent changes in the IRS after congressional scrutiny have resulted in what some are calling a 'new attitude' at the IRS with respect to audits & other functions. This supposed shift it away from 'enforcement '-- under which 'examinations' are one of the primary tools -- and toward 'customer service' -- which, ostensibly, means that even in those situations where an audit or examination is conducted, the emphasis will be on fair, timely & equitable service, rather than on collections}.

A quick look at the figures shows that, on average, for individual 1040 returns {excluding those filing Schedule C or Schedule F -- for farm-related income}, those filing a Schedule C were more than 2.5 times [2.73 times] more likely to be 'examined'. What is more interesting, however, is that, even for those reporting incomes under $25,000, those filing Schedule C with Total Gross Receipts [TGR] under $25,000 were still 2.3 times more likely to be audited, even though TGR is likely to be greater than actual profit or income.

For those reporting incomes above $100,000 vs. those filing a Schedule C with a TGR above $100,000, those filing the Schedule C were 1.8 times more likely to be audited. Finally, for those reporting incomes between $25,000- $100,000 vs. those filing a Schedule C with a TGR between $25,000- $100,000, those filing the Schedule C were 3.5 times more likely to be audited.

The figures appear here in Table 2 {above or left, depending upon your browser's resolution}.

It's probably also worthwhile to take a quick glance at the 'Recommended Additional Tax & Penalties'. While it isn't possible to draw precise figures, we can determine some figures.

* The Recommended Additional Tax & Penalties [RATP] for those filing a Schedule C with Total Gross Receipts [TGR] below $25,000 amounted to more than 3.8 times what would appear to be their proportional share when compared to general 1040 returns reporting income of $25,000 or less.

* The RTAP for those filing a Schedule C with Total Gross Receipts [TGR] above $100,000 was 2.37 times the proportional share when compared to general 1040 returns reporting income levels above $100,000. ... and

* For those filing a Schedule C reporting between $25,000-$100,000 TGR, their proportional share was 6.3 times greater.

While there is no certain way to gauge these figures based on the data available in these tables, the fact remains that, for all income levels ...

  • Schedule C returns were consistently more likely to be examined ...
  • Schedule C returns with Total Gross Receipts under $25,000 were more than 4 times as likely to be audited than those with Total Personal Incomes between $50,000 - $100,000, and
  • 40% more likely to be audited than those with Total Personal Incomes above $100,000 ... and finally
  • Schedule C returns were consistently more likely to result in greater amounts of Recommended Additional Tax & Penalties
