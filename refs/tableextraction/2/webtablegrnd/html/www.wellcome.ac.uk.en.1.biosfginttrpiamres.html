Results and discussion

>

Funding

The total identifiable global expenditure on malaria research in 1993 was approximately US$84 million (1992 base year). Over half the total came from the USA (especially the US Agency for International Development, USAID, and the National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases, NIAID). Much of the remainder came from Europe (notably the Wellcome Trust and the Medical Research Council, MRC, in the UK), and from the Programme in Research and Training in Tropical Diseases (TDR) which is co-sponsored by the United Nations Development Programme, the World Bank and the World Health Organization (WHO), as well as several governments, especially in Scandinavia. Funding has declined in some organizations over the last decade, most notably in USAID. However, there have been increases elsewhere, especially at the Wellcome Trust, NIAID and the US Department of Defense. > Global investment in malaria research over the past ten years has been very low compared with other disease areas, and appears to be declining further (see figure 1). Expressed as investment in research per death, malaria research - at approximately US$42 per fatal case - receives less funding, by one or two orders of magnitude, than other diseases such as cancer, HIV/AIDS or asthma.



Research outputs

International publishing activity was lower than in other biomedical fields reflecting the relatively low funding input. The USA is currently the largest single contributor to the malaria research literature (34 per cent of the global total in 1994), but its world share of publications has declined over the past ten years (from 42 per cent in 1984). Meanwhile, the UK share increased, from 14 per cent of the world total in 1984 to 18 per cent in 1994. The world share of malaria publications also increased for France, Australia and Thailand. International co-authorship was fostered by collaborative research centres located in malaria-endemic countries; 38 per cent of UK output was internationally co-authored, largely reflecting work at the overseas units of the Wellcome Trust in Thailand, Kenya and Vietnam, as well as the MRC unit in The Gambia. Co-authorship was also high between UK researchers and scientists in Malawi and Papua New Guinea.



Citations largely reflected funding - the six largest contributors to malaria research had more citations to papers than other organizations. The proportion of USAID papers having a high impact decreased between 1984 and 1989. Papers acknowledging the NIAID and the MRC appeared to have the greatest citation impact.



Activity in different subfields

Malaria research was subdivided into 14 subfields. ‘Clinical’ research papers - describing studies of malaria in humans, ranging from descriptions of clinical management and pathophysiology to human immune responses and genetic susceptibility - accounted for 42 per cent of the world total. The remainder were ‘non-clinical’ papers - studies of malaria in animal models or in vitro and basic studies on mosquito vectors.



International publication activity over the last decade was evenly distributed across most categories (see figure 2). Publications were most numerous in two major research categories: (1) clinical treatment and pathophysiology; and (2) basic science studies of the parasite. > Research into antimalarial drugs, immunology, epidemiology and mosquito vectors were also well represented by publications. There were fewer publications concerning intervention trials and health services research in malaria-endemic countries.



On the basis of the address of the first author only, the USA dominated all subfields except those relating to clinical medicine and pathophysiology. UK authors published most frequently in pathophysiology and disease symptoms, while Thailand was the world leader in clinical management of malaria and drug trials. High co-authorship between researchers in the UK and Thailand suggests that the UK also had an important role in this area.



The Wellcome Trust portfolio

Papers acknowledging the Wellcome Trust and published in 1984, 1989 and 1994 were reviewed by 13 subfield experts. Reviewers felt that the Wellcome Trust’s research outputs made a contribution both to advancing basic knowledge, and to improving malaria treatment and control. Realization of practical benefits from Trust-funded research was expected largely within five years of publication.



Publication output in different subfields generally reflected funding inputs but publications were low relative to expenditure in three subfields: (1) immunology and vaccine development; (2) epidemiology; and (3) intervention trials and health services research. Conversely, outputs were high relative to inputs for studies on both the biology and the biochemistry of the parasite.



Current practice and future directions of malaria research

A total of 115 individuals - including researchers, clinicians, health services managers and practitioners, industrialists and administrators in both malaria-endemic and non-endemic countries - responded to an international survey on current practice and future directions in malaria research.



Parasite genetics and biology were identified most frequently as having very good prospects for making substantial advances in knowledge over the next five years (see figure 3). > Intervention trials and health services research were considered to be very under-resourced. Investment was also encouraged in a number of other areas: (1) basic studies of the parasite; (2) epidemiology; (3) immunology and vaccine development; and (4) antimalarial drug development.



A large number of respondents had personal experience of the practical application of research results. However, respondents also identified a number of key obstacles to the uptake of research results, including: (1) insufficient orientation of research programmes to public needs in malaria-endemic countries; (2) inadequate standardization of field techniques; (3) inadequate assessment of research results and development of applications; and (4) resource limitations in malaria-endemic countries.



A number of measures for tackling the perceived barriers were identified: (1) renew efforts to promote active communication across professional and national boundaries; (2) standardize methods in field trials; (3) increase investment in operational research; (4) widen dissemination of research results; (5) establish a centre or centres to prepare, maintain and disseminate systematic reviews of the results of malaria research, and to develop new products from the results of research that would contribute to the treatment and control of malaria; and (6) create more training opportunities for researchers based in malaria-endemic countries.
