Introduction

The Australian Institute of Criminology is a unique national organisation, the work of which aims to explore, describe and explain issues of public policy significance in crime prevention and criminal justice.

The AIC conducts in-house research and also brings together researchers and practitioners to work co-operatively in building onto our knowledge base.

The Institute's work is communicated to government, media and the public through its conferences, seminars and workshops, and publications. This conference is one means of exploring and communicating ideas and strategies about a significant issue of concern to the public, namely crime mapping.

The Institute's crime analysis and mapping system was established in late 1999. It utilises geographical information system (GIS) technology to examine the spatial distribution and displacement of crime over time, to attain improved knowledge in the field of crime mapping and analysis and its application in crime prevention practice and policy.

The National Key Centre for Social Applications of GIS provides research and training in spatial information, demographic analysis and community planning using the technology of geographical information systems (GIS). The core partners of the centre are the University of Adelaide, Flinders University, University of South Australia, the Department of Transport, Urban Planning and the Arts, the Department of the Environment and the Australian Bureau of Statistics. At GISCA, cutting edge spatial information technology is applied and utilised to achieve a better understanding of important social, economic and demographic issues and also to produce more efficient, equitable and profitable provision of resources and services by all levels of government and the private sector. The objectives of GISCA are:

  • To provide a major focus and vehicle for researchers and planners to collaborate in the analysis and use of population and social data.
  • To make public and private sector planning in Australia 'smarter' especially in resource allocation issues, by using GIS technology to analyse spatial data in ways not previously possible.
  • To undertake basic and applied research in the use of social and demographic data using GIS technology.
  • To develop new technologies in GIS.
  • To provide education and training in the use of social and demographic data in planning.

Aims and Objectives

The conference aims to discuss current and potential uses of computerised mapping and associated GIS technologies in adding value to public policy and practice in the fields of crime prevention and crime control. It will be organised around 3 major topics:

  • Mapping for crime prevention,
  • Mapping for crime control (policing, police operations and police investigations), and
  • Mapping for regional comparisons of crime.

This will be an important forum of interest to policy makers, local governments, police officers and crime prevention practitioners, GIS professionals and social scientists.

Content

This workshop will give participants an overview of Geographical Information Systems (GIS), demonstrate the usefulness of GIS for the spatial analysis of crime data, and give participants hands on experience in the use of two freely available Crime Analysis Packages. Participants will have access to both MapInfo 5.5 or ArcView 3.2 and the workshop will finish with a discussion of the benefits and limitations of both software systems.
