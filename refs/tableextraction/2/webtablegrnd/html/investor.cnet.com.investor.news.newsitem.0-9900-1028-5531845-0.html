Bank Stocks, Bonds Fall on Pacific G&E Bankruptcy (Update3)

4/6/01 1:16 PM

Source:Bloomberg News

New York, April 6 (Bloomberg) -- Shares of Bank of America Corp., J.P. Morgan Chase & Co., and Bank One Corp., lenders to Pacific Gas & Electric, fell after the unit of California's largest utility filed for bankruptcy protection.

Bank of America fell $2.26, or 4.4 percent, to $49.59 after trading as high as $52.50. J.P. Morgan Chase fell $2.11, or almost 5 percent, to $40.39, after trading as high as $42.50. Bank One fell $1.29, or 3.7 percent, to $33.61.

''It's too early to determine if there will be any impact on earnings,'' said Andrew Collins, who follows J.P. Morgan and other bank stocks for ING Barings. ''It will show as a credit issue and probably have an impact on non-performing loans.''

According to Bloomberg data, Bank of America, the No. 2 U.S. business lender, had arranged $2.15 billion of loans for Edison, PG&E and their subsidiaries as of January, including a $1 billion loan for PG&E Corp. in October and an $850 million loan to Pacific Gas & Electric in December.

The bank also arranged a $300 million loan in May for Edison Mission Energy, a unit of Edison International.

''Loans to companies in bankruptcy do not automatically result in a certain level of loss,'' said Shirley Norton, a Bank of America spokeswoman. ''We are quite capable of dealing with this development.''

Other Lenders

J.P. Morgan, the biggest U.S. lender, had led $2.02 billion of loans outstanding to PG&E and Edison as of January. The bank arranged an $818 million facility to Edison in May, and led $650 million of $1.675 billion in loans to PG&E subsidiary US Generating Co. in 1998. It also arranged a $550 million loan to PG&E Generating Co. in August.

Chicago-based Bank One arranged a $203 million backup line for Pacific Gas & Electric last April.

Bank bonds also fell. Bank of America's 7.4 percent notes due in 2011 were bid at 1.89 percentage points more than benchmark U.S. Treasuries, up from 1.8 percentage points this morning, traders said. Bond prices move inverse to yield.

Pacific Gas & Electric is a unit of PG&E Corp., which together with Edison International said they are more than $14 billion in debt from buying electricity at rates far higher than regulators will let them charge customers.

The drop in bank stocks and bonds reflects ''the chance Edison will do the same thing'' as well as the PG&E bankruptcy, ING's Collins said.

J.P. Morgan spokeswoman Leigh Pierce declined to comment, as did Bank One spokesman Tom Kelly.

First Mortgage Bonds

Ambac Financial Group Inc. has guaranteed $68.7 million of Pacific Gas & Electric secured first mortgage bonds, the bond insurer said in a statement.

Interest payments on those bonds total about $4.7 million annually, and the utility hasn't missed any payments, Ambac said. None of the principal matures until 2009, when $1.4 million is due, it added.

Kent Harvey, PG&E's chief financial officer, said in a conference call that interest payments on secured first mortgage bonds will be made during bankruptcy, and any missed principal paid when reorganization approved. No principal or interest payments will be made to unsecured creditors until a reorganization is approved.

As a precaution, Ambac said it reserved $5 million for California utility exposure last December. The company also insures $75.1 million of Southern California Edison first mortgage bonds, which have a lien on utility assets.

Ambac shares fell $2.98 to $59.97 in late afternoon trading.

Exposure

MBIA Inc. said MBIA-insured bondholders ''will receive all their principal and interest payments as scheduled,'' said Neil Budnick, its chief financial officer, in a statement.

The company has direct net par exposure to Pacific Gas & Electric of approximately $590 million, of which about 61 percent are secured by first mortgage bonds with the remainder being senior unsecured bonds.
