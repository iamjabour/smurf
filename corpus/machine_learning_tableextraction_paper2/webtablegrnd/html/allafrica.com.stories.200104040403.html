Accra Mail (Accra)

ANALYSIS

> April 4, 2001

> Posted to the web April 4, 2001

Anthony Korsah Dick

> Accra

Ever since independence, the Civil Service in Ghana has been the focus of much critical attention and seldom of praise. In spite of the ever growing measures at reform, the Service continues to draw fire for all manner of shortcomings and weaknesses - for inefficiency, incompetence, sycophancy, pen- pushing, red-tapeism, facelessness, corruption - and the list goes on.

At the purely administrative and organisational level, most of the shortcomings in the Service have over the years been highlighted in various studies within the Service itself and more importantly, on the initiative of successive governments in response to the felt need for reform. As one former Head of the Service once remarked, the Service has been "investigated, dissected and subjected to various changes in structure, functions and operational codes all hopefully designed to ensure a better and satisfactory performance".

And yet, quite in spite of the rather considerable improvement achieved, the basic dissatisfaction with the Service, by and large, still persists and the search, it seems, will continue until the changes desired are achieved in a more reasonable and acceptable measure.

Barely two weeks ago, the Civil Service was once again in the glare of public concern when in a news item carried in the Daily Graphic, the Head of Civil Service, Dr. Dodoo, was reported to have challenged the action of the President, Mr. J.A. Kufuor, asking him (Dr. Dodoo) to proceed on leave and hand over to his Chief Director. Dr. Dodoo, according to the report, had written to the President to "state his case and copied his lawyers for the necessary action on his concerns."

Not quite two days later, the searchlight was on the Service yet again - and even more fiercely this time - when in a letter carried in The Statesman, a contributor identified simply as a Civil Servant advanced a number of arguments calling on the government to "sack all those Chief Directors". In the view of the contributor, it was abundantly clear:

1. That the position of Chief Director was political and not an automatic extension of the regular Civil Service posts;

2. That the appointment "explicitly demanded unalloyed political allegiance"; and, even more damning

3. That many of the incumbent officers had seriously campaigned in the offices for the retention of the NDC in power.

It would appear, at least on the strength of the evidence against the Chief Directors, that if hitherto, the general and persistent dissatisfaction with the Civil Service had been predicated largely on the structural, procedural and other such weaknesses in the Service, now, thanks to the charge against the Chief Directors, the spectre of partisan politics or the politicisation of the service has raised an issue of grave consequence that cannot be ignored.

As a rule and general observation, the Civil Service has since independence been known and acknowledged to be non-political; this time-honoured tradition of neutrality has rarely, if ever, been called into serious question on a scale demanding critical national attention. In this attribute, civil servants have traditionally been required to serve impartially and to best of their ability whatever the government in power without any untoward display of partisanship or compromise to their personal, political belief. Thus, even though in the jargon of the Service, the Civil Servant was supposed to be an extension of the Minister's personality, it had always been understood, nonetheless, that this perception was essentially only a figure of speech designed to underscore the dedication and loyalty required of the Civil Servant in the conduct of government business.

Again, as our experience since independence clearly shows, even where outsiders or men of achievement in business, academia and elsewhere have been selectively appointed into the Service, the emphasis invariably has been on competence and the need to enhance management capacity at the top. As I recall, this was how such highly regarded individuals like H.P. Nelson came to be appointed into the Service. Rarely, until now, has partisan politics ever featured in any reform of the Service as an issue of any fundamental and crucial significance.

A random sampling of the reform measures in the Service should help to illustrate the point of this observation. If we take it all the way back to pre- independence times, it would be recalled that the modern Civil Service in this country began when thanks to the efforts of Governor Guggisberg, the first organised plan for the development of an indigenous Civil Service was launched in 1925-6. Other initiatives followed like the Capt. Lynch Report of 1944 on grades within government departments; the Harragin Commission of 1945 which advocated two separate councils for junior and senior staff consultations; and the interim Public Service Commission set up in 1948 to advise on appointments and promotions within the Service.

I stand to be corrected but beginning from the fifties to the present day, one can count well over 25 initiatives in the form of commissions and committees set up one after the other all aimed at improving specific areas of the Service as it grew and matured.

Thus, for example, the Lidbury Commission was set up in 1950-51 to undertake a detailed and comprehensive review of the organisation of government machinery; the Bourne Commission in 1955 to advise on suitable measures for the devolution of powers to the regions leading to the beginnings of decentralisation in the country; the Civil Service Rules and Regulations Committee in 1964, 1966 and 1970 to revise and update administrative instructions in conformity with changes in public administration; the Mills-Odoi Commission in 1967 to review the structure of the Civil Service in its entirety including remuneration, the decentralisation of administrative functions and the reorganisation of regional, district and local administrations; and the Okoh Commission on the structure procedures and methods of the Civil Service which submitted its report in December 1976.

Remarkably in one single year, 1982 alone, the Civil Service witnessed the emergence of no less than five key committees, namely the Kaku Kyiamah Committee on the Restructuring of Ministerial Organisation; the Kufuor Committee (does it ring a bell?) on the decentralisation of national administration; the Sackey Committee also on the Restructuring of Ministerial Organisation; the Asamoah Committee on the decentralisation of national administration, organisation and structures; and the Sowu Committee to produce guidelines for programming the organisation and development of the district as the unit of planning and programme co-ordination. The proposals of these committees were put together in a consolidated report by the all-embracing Public Administration Restructuring and Decentralisation Implementation Committee, PARDIC, set up in 1983.

Additionally, deficiencies in such other areas like management and supervisory capacity and, in particular, training, have since also been addressed in phases under a package of proposals, namely, the Civil Service Reform Programme, developed with the collaboration of the World Bank and the British Overseas Development Agency, ODA.

As the pre-occupation with issues of structure, procedure, operational codes remuneration, rules and regulations and the like would appear to show, never in the long and dynamic history of reforms in the Ghana Civil Service has partisan politics been raised to such a pitch of concern as we see in the charge against the Chief Directors; never, as reports amply testify, have Civil Servants been known to mix politics so brazenly with their work, even to the point of donning party colours to public functions. This issue is of fundamental significance in view of the crucial need for us to achieve a harmonious reconciliation of the dual mission of the Civil Service, namely, the role of the Service as a political instrument and its mission as a professional institution.

If I may stay with this observation for a moment, it seems to me that three options are open to us in this regard. The first is an emphasis on the Service as a political instrument. This, ironically, is exemplified by the experience in the former Soviet Union and satellite countries of Eastern Europe as well as the United States of America where it is pejoratively referred to as the 'spoils' system. The second is a bias for the professional roles such as we have in Britain. And the third is a blend of the two as we see, for instance, in France.

The evidence is that we have not as a nation settled on a choice even though the trend hitherto, would appear to point to a bias for the professional role. If, as it appears, the answer is ultimately destined to emerge as a function of the political evolutionary process, it is important, nonetheless, that we keep this issue consciously in mind as we continue to look for solutions to the practical shortcomings and weaknesses in the Service. The case of Dr. Dodoo and the Chief Directors has now brought the issue fully into the open and will, doubtless, demand its share of attention in a national debate. For now the question still remains: What kind of Civil Service do we want? Are we ready to settle on a choice as between the different roles of the Service in the conduct of government business and national development?

The Ghana Civil Service has earned praise before under men of stature like A.L. Adu, Michael Dei-Anang and Chapman-Nyaho. Others like Fred Arkhurst and E. M. Debrah are known to have overcome political pressure in dignity and style; they spurned the attractions of political office and left the Service with their integrity intact. The Ghana Civil Service will earn praise yet again.
