Forex Auction Should Bring About Positive Results This Time



>

>

The Times of Zambia (Lusaka)

March 22, 2001

> Posted to the web March 22, 2001

Chilufya Sampa

> Lusaka

The recently introduced auctioning of foreign currency by the Bank of Zambia has generated intense debate encompassing wide ranging views about both the effectiveness or lack of it among various stake holders.

This is not the first time that such measures, which are aimed at stabilising the Kwacha against the major foreign currencies of the world, have been evoked.

A similar initiative was put in place during the second republic, although this is the first time such a decision has been made in a liberalised environment.

The BOZ has assured all interested parties that the newly introduced foreign exchange auction is not a foreign exchange control and will not lead to that.

It is not a secret that Zambia depends on foreign donor aid and has little international reserves. It is for this reason that the country cannot prop up its currency and has allowed market forces to determine the level of the Kwacha against other currencies.

However the auction system seems to stabilise the fall of the Kwacha because mere speculators creating artificial demand may have caused the decline.

After independence Zambia had enough foreign reserves and could afford to peg or fix the local Kwacha to the dollar. But because the dollar was undervalued, (propping up the local currency creates demand because it implies that more people can afford to purchase the dollar), the result was that there was a run on the dollar depleting the foreign reserves that the central bank may have had.

Hence the introduction of a flexible exchange system in the 1990s was to allow market forces determine the price level of the foreign currencies given the laws of supply and demand subject to Government intervention.

And as it is well known little supply of foreign exchange implies that the price is high because demand is high.

The events last year when the Kwacha was falling every week, against the major currencies of the world showed that the foreign exchange market was not in equilibrium, a situation where demand of the foreign exchange outweighed the supply.

This is the development, which led the Government to introduce measures to stabilise the market.

The auction system is one such intervention mainly aimed at reducing speculation on the market. This will require the support of the main stakeholders or foreign exchange earners to compile with the laid down rules for its success.

Fortunately it seems the stakeholders are working with Government from the positive results so far exhibited.

The difference between the last time the Central Bank used the auction system and now is that foreign exchange controls were prominent in the second republic.

Dr Joseph Kapayi in his doctoral thesis said that the auctioning of foreign currency then did not work well as the Kwacha continued to plunge against major world currencies immediately after the auction was introduced forcing the Government to stop the auction later.

However, this time around, going by the preliminary results the auction seems to be heading towards a positive out come, at least based on the three weeks of implementation of the auction system.

In the period since auctioning was implemented the exchange rate fluctuations, up or down movements of the value of the Kwacha have been very minimal. The fluctuations have in total usually amounted to not more than K100.

For example this week the dollar is selling at K3,475.17 and buying at K3,407.03 while last week it was selling at K3,476.91 and buying at K3,408.74 f rom the BOZ.

However, certain sectors of society are still uncertain of what this auction will bring while others are more optimistic and calling for more measures to be implemented.

Mr. George Chisanga, a banking and legal expert, is calling for the statutory instrument on the auction to have punitive action against those who fail to comply with the rules of the auction and other measures set by the BOZ.

To be able to punish businesses for non compliance would be to suggest that Zambia has introduced foreign exchange controls.

The route the BOZ has taken of having dialogue with the main stakeholders in the economy and making an impression on the need to have a stable Kwacha is one way of avoiding controls and unfavourable donor support.

The stakeholders would make more profits in a favourable environment and using this as a main factor the stakeholders have shown a willingness to comply with the measures. Therefore calls for punitive actions may be a little bit premature.

The auction system is just a measure to stabilise the Kwacha on the foreign market against other world currencies.

From the results shown thus far, it can be assumed that all stakeholders in this market are adhering to Government measures.

The results show that the Kwacha has gained in value since the measures were implemented from K4,600 in January 2001 to K3,400 against the American dollar in March 2001. And because the price of foreign exchange is being determined through the auctioning at the Central Bank - market forces determine the cost price of foreign exchange.

The question could be whether the new auction system will actually bring the foreign exchange market to equilibrium where price stability in the foreign exchange market will be maintained without controls.

Markets in any economy strive to maintain stability or equilibrium and when this is affected by a shock, be it internal or external, the market should adjust and return to equilibrium.

It should however be noted that controls in any market distort the market and usually efficiency is lost. Hence controls are a cost to an economy.

Against this backdrop, where the country does not have foreign exchange reserves and has a small export base the auction seems to be a good interim measure while the productive sectors pull their socks.

The dilemma Zambia may be in is that it has to ensure that the free market economy is prevalent to attract donor support and foreign direct investment while at the same time maintain a hold on foreign exchange rates and inflation levels.

Whatever the Central Bank does should not erode the confidence of the business community.

The ultimate aim for the country should be to increase the export base and export earnings and achieve the targeted international reserves of US $150 million set in this year's budget.

Dr George Chabwera an expert in the commerce and industrial sectors said that Zambia's woes in the foreign exchange market will end when the productive sectors of agriculture and manufacturing industry increase their export capacity and earnings. But before then a stable Kwacha seems possible with the auction system.
