Firecats: Simmons' job is to score

Wednesday, April 4, 2001

By WILL GRAVES, wrgraves@naplesnews.com

David Simmons' job title says "offensive specialist." His job description is a little more specific: "Score."

As the only non-quarterback on the Florida Firecat offense not required to also play defense, Simmons recipe for success when he gets his hands on the ball is simple: catch the ball, find the end zone, repeat as desired.

Simmons and company expect to reach the end zone so often this season they hope the scoreboard operator at TECO Arena develops a mild case of carpal tunnel syndrome trying to keep up.

"We'll be exciting," said Simmons, who played with the Florida Bobcats of the Arena Football League. "We've got the best player's coach in the league in Coach (Jim) Jensen. We've got a good quarterback, an accurate kicker and a good defense who can bring the wood. I think we can compete and hopefully make a run at the playoffs."

The Firecats will play in the af2's seven-team Southeast Division along with their expansion brethren, the Macon Knights and the Columbus Wardogs. The trio will join the Pensacola Barracudas, Jacksonville Tomcats, Tallahassee Thunder and returning division champion Augusta Stallions.

The high turnover rate on af2 rosters combined with sketchy scouting reports leaves Jensen hesitant to speculate on the talent level of other clubs. The way Jensen sees it, if he can get his 18 guys to learn the game and play together, they'll be just fine.

"I think we'll be OK," Jensen said. "Talking to some of the officials who worked our intrasquad scrimmage, they've seen Jacksonville and they think we'll line up pretty well with them. They've got a good-size line but so do we."

Jason Cue, who set numerous passing records at NCAA Division I-AA Wagner College while leading the school to a national title in 1994, will be the starting quarterback when the Firecats open their inaugural season Friday night in Jacksonville.

Cue, 27, bounced around football for four seasons after graduating before taking some time off to open a carpeting business in New Jersey. When the chance to play for Jensen came up, he couldn't pass it up.

"He's picking up the offense really quickly," Jensen said. "He's a veteran and he's got a good, accurate arm. He's been around and he'll give us some leadership. He makes good decisions and he's got the talent."

Former Clarion University quarterback Chris Weibel, 26, will serve as Cue's backup. Weibel was to have battled Chris Perkins for the backup job, but Perkins abruptly left the team following last week's intrasquad scrimmage. Weibel has looked solid in practice as he continues to grasp Jensen's intricate offensive system.

"Weibel is coming along," Jensen said. "Really I think both our quarterbacks will do fine. Jason is the guy right now and with the receivers and guys we have around them on offense, we'll put some points on the board. I hope we can average 50-60 points a game. The goal is to score every time we get our hands on the ball."

Cue and Weibel will have an athletic corp of receivers to get the ball to, including 6-foot-2, 200-pound Terrence Hatton. Hatton will have a definite size advantage over most af2 defensive backs and has the speed to make them respect the deep ball.

"Terrence and the rest of the guys run good routes," Jensen said. "They're not making a lot of mental mistakes. With them we'll have the ability to mix it up so the other team doesn't know what to expect."

Anthony Dixon and Corey Foreman have also shone well in the early going. Dixon, a former standout at Estero High, may have the best hands on the team while Foreman has the highest vertical leap on the team.

David Simmons will handle the kick return duties.

"We'll be fast, even by arena standards," Jensen said. "With David and Terrence back there, that gives us some experience and that'll help, especially early in the season."

The Firecats may have the strongest, if not most talented, fullback/linebacking duo in the division. Blake McCollom, 6-2, 255 played with the Charleston (S.C.) Swamp Foxes last year while Brad Dunn (5-11, 250) played for Tallahassee.

McCollom has been solid during camp as Dunn recovers from a broken bone in his right wrist. Leroy Thompson is steadily improving, and could stay with the team early in the season while Dunn heals.

"It's been one of our most competitive positions in camp," Jensen said. "We're lucky that we have some versatility there where we can move guys around."

The offensive and defensive linemen are big and quick. At 6-3, 325, Rufus White is the biggest Firecat and will be one of the bookends. White's experience with the af2's Tulsa Talons last season should help. Fort Myers High graduate Rick Nipper surprised even Jensen in winning the starting center/nose tackle spot.

"Nipper's done a great job," Jensen said. "Rufus is a veteran and we've got other guys like (Patrick) Russo and Miguel (Gonzalez) who are athletic, big guys."

Russo, 25, spent four years chasing down quarterbacks playing nose guard at the University of Connecticut. Now, forced to play offensive line too, he's finding himself turning into a quarterback's best friend.

"If you're a true defensive player, and you play in the arena league, you have to learn to love offense," Russo said. "Because here, if you want to play defense and get out there after the quarterback, you'll have to learn to protect that quarterback."

The Firecats should have no problem defensively. Russo, Odell Parks and Chris McIntosh have been so quick in practice at times Jensen has asked them to lay off in the middle of plays so the offense can execute the play from start to finish.

"We'll be like the (Baltimore) Ravens," said Parks, 28, who earned a Super Bowl ring playing special teams for the Dallas Cowboys in 1995. "We just have to get the offensive line together, get 'em on the same page. Defensively, I think we have a chance to be really good."

Good by arena standards, however, is a little different than good by outdoor standards. While holding a team to less than 10 points in the outdoor game is a hallmark of a good defense, in af2 holding an opponent to less than 30 points is a more realistic goal.

"The new guys will have to find out that holding them to a field goal is almost as good as a full stop," Parks said. "You still want to keep them out of the end zone as much as possible, but this is arena football. There's a lot of scoring. We just have to make sure we keep our heads in the game if a team puts a couple of scores on the board."

Isaac Harvin, Rodrick Burnett and Montain Murray will draw af2's most thankless job, defensive specialist. Two defensive specialists (think a combination of cornerback/safety) replace the offensive specialists when teams switch from offense to defense.

Harvin, Burnett and Murray job is simple yet difficult: stop the opposition's best receivers. Af2 rules are little help. In af2, one offensive player can be in forward motion at the snap, giving them a head start on the defensive back. Firecat defensive coordinator Riley Ware's biggest concern is making sure his specialists stay upbeat when the going gets rough.

"You're going to get beat, but you have to get back in the fight," Ware said. "It's really hard with the guys in motion to stop them all the time. You get beat three times, that's 21 points. But in arena football, 21 points isn't that much. In arena football, it's going to take a lot more to beat you."

Bonita Springs resident Phil Setterquist will handle the kicking duties, and should be pretty busy considering the Firecats will be in field goal range on every possession.

"Phil's done a great job in practice," Jensen said. "He's been pretty accurate. Kick the ball straight and you'll be OK. I'm just looking forward to getting out there and seeing how we'll do."
