Update: In December, we picked seven funds that could provide shelter from a storm. They came through for investors, and now we may need them more than ever.



Back in December, when the economy was showing its first real signs of slowing and most of the stocks that led the bull market were getting whacked, we suggested seven "foul-weather funds" that could shelter a portfolio in rough times.

And Nor'easters aside, the weather has indeed been rough on Wall Street these past three months. The S&P 500 index is down nearly 6 percent, and the Nasdaq, which in December was already 50 percent off its peak, has lost another 18 percent.

We revisited our foul-weather funds to see how they've fared, and whether they still make sense.

For the three-month period from November 30 to March 2, you would have lost money in only one of the funds: Brandywine . Though the mid-cap growth fund rightly kept a low tech exposure, some of its top non-tech holdings disappointed in January, including Boeing and Safeway . Our two other stock funds also beat the market, but Clipper was the real star, with a 9.8 percent gain. Tweedy, Browne Global Value held its ground, rising 1.4 percent.

All three funds are solid additions to your portfolio, in good times and bad. Indeed, says Morningstar's director of fund analysis, Russ Kinnel, the funds selected "are good at protecting against a big loss. But they also have upside. You don't want your defenses so great that they kill you in a bull market."

Not surprisingly, four of our suggestions were bond funds. While the average U.S. stock fund has posted a loss of more than 4 percent in the past three months, the typical bond fund was up about the same amount, according to data from Morningstar.

Harbor Bond , which is run by Bill Gross, one of the best bond fund managers around, did the least well relative to its peers during the three-month period, which is not to say that you wouldn't have made money (it was up 3.5 percent). But that underperformance is due in part to the fund's concentration in high-quality credit, as opposed to far riskier junk and emerging market bonds, which have done well recently, says Morningstar analyst Eric Jacobson.
