Owners hire management to do one thing: Increase the value of their company.

If you think that management has a different purpose, just try telling your shareholders at the next annual meeting or the analysts on your next conference call that you have other things to do besides increasing value.

There are only five ways to increase the value of a company. There are countless specific strategies, investment options, and decisions facing management, but they all fall into one or more of these categories. These actions can be applied at the corporate level or at each individual SBU. I recommend that each unit's Strategic Plan contain a section that addresses these five methods of increasing value. Please see The Value Curve section to ensure you know what value is and why one wants to increase it.

Five Ways To Increase Value

0.

First, Protect the Value You Have, Then:

1. Make Profitable New Investments

NPV > $0, IRR > After tax cost of capital.

2.

Improve Current Business Units

Increase profitability: Increase sales, and/or lower costs.

Reduce required investment: Minimize Net Operating Assets.

Let RONA be your guide.

3.

Shut Down Or Divest Business Units That Are Destroying Value

A reverse investment: Shut down or sell a unit if NPV of Selling > NPV of Operating.

4.

Lower The After Tax Cost Of Capital

Use lowest cost financing, debt vs. equity.

Increase the confidence of investors and analysts--be credible.

Earnings growth with lower volatility--no surprises.

5.

Lower The Tax Rates of Our Country

This not-so-simple task would add more value to your company (and to your personal wealth) than anything you could accomplish in 1 - 4 above.

That's It! Everything you have ever done or will do in your company falls into one or more of these categories.

Discussion

Five Ways To Increase Value

0. First, Protect the Value You Have (The financial Hippocratic oath)

This is the foundation of value creation. Unfortunately, this simple self evident premise is often overlooked. When you invest cash in a new investment you are using value that was created in prior investments or given to you by investors. The risk of new investments should not be taken lightly and should be the major focus of new investment ideas. Remember, most corporations only earn about 5% on sales. This means that for every dollar you waste you must generate $20 of new sales just to get back to even. If your new idea ends up wasting $10 million an additional $200 million in Sales is required just to get your shareholders back to where they were. You should first worry about the return of your investment instead of the return on your investment. This is known as "Risk Analysis."
1. Make Profitable New Investments
This sounds simple and it is--financially speaking. The challenge is coming up with the project idea in the first place and then executing it. There is no mystery in whether a given investment idea, with a particular set of assumptions, will increase value or destroy it. That is the purpose of proper investment analysis --ensuring that the decision makers understand all of the relevant assumptions that are in a particular investment scenario. Making new investments with NPV > $0 (IRR > A-tax Cost of Capital) increases the value of the corporation assuming that the risk profile and maximum cash outlay are acceptable. The hard part is executing the project so that the actual cash flows are as predicted or better.
2. Improve Current Business Units
This method can be a lot of fun and very rewarding. You already have a business unit up and running so you can now turn your focus on improving its operations to keep it generating value for your shareholders, satisfying your customers, and frustrating competitors. You can increase the value of a business unit by increasing cash profits or by reducing net assets (i.e., the shareholder's investment). Profits are increased by increasing sales and/or lowering costs. Net assets can be reduced by shedding plant and equipment and/or improving the cash conversion cycle (lowering net working capital).
3. Shut Down Or Divest Business Units That Are Destroying Value
This is probably the most hated and overlooked way of increasing value. Any suggestion of disposing of assets or whole business units is usually met with severe hostility because it implies that a bad investment decision was made previously. This is often the case but also conditions may have changed causing a once profitable investment to turn bad. Proper investment analysis is unemotional and will warn management that they have assets or whole business units that are destroying value instead of increasing it. This may not be pleasant, but it is essential to admit mistakes and correct them before they consume shareholder's wealth. Each business unit or project contributes--for better or worse--to the company's ROA and therefore, each should be monitored continuously and actively managed.
4. Lower The After Tax Cost Of Capital
Lowering the corporation's cost of capital increases value by not only lowering the actual financing expenses, but more importantly, by increasing the value of all of its future cash flows. This is usually considered the responsibility of the treasurer, but all of the SBU's can play a role in decreasing the corporation's cost of capital. Obviously, the treasury department should find the lowest cost financing that keeps the corporation solvent. The SBU's can also do their part in lowering the cost of capital by consistently delivering growing earnings without unduly adding to the investment base. This gives the corporation credibility in the markets and lowers the investor's required risk premium.
5. Lower The Tax Rates of Our Country
Lowering our country's tax rates would increase the value of all companies, large and small, and increase the wealth of all citizens--immediately and dramatically. This simple concept appears to be impossible to implement in our increasingly socialistic country. Also, I am shocked at how many CEO's and others in top management vote to harm their corporations given this known link between taxes and value destruction. Take any NPV/IRR analysis that you have done and watch what happens to NPV when you lower the tax rate to 5% vs. the current 38%. This staggering increase in value is real and given it is readily demonstrable it proves that anyone who defends our current tax system and its high rates is either ignorant or evil.
