Big donors score best MSU seats

Corporate connections ensure tickets to see Spartans hoops

March 15, 2001

BY JIM SCHAEFER

FREE PRESS STAFF WRITER

Michigan State University sophomore Melissa Klink couldn't get a season ticket to see the Spartans play basketball this year.

TICKET TALLY
Here's a rundown of corporate season ticket holders.

But Coca-Cola could. So could Meijer. Same story with Kmart, Old Kent Bank and Gov. John Engler's office.

Capitalizing on a championship year has been good for the university, which saw athletic contributions soar. But perhaps not so good for students and the general buying public.

As the Spartans packed Wednesday and headed for another NCAA tournament, they closed out a sellout season at the Breslin Center, where donations and corporate connections got a lot of people in the door.

In the 15,000-seat arena, all but the 100 seats for visiting teams were sold this year as season tickets. Students got 3,000; companies owned 1,200, and individuals held 10,700.

The university refused to divulge details about who the big-ticket holders are among individuals, claiming that would invade their privacy and unfairly suggest how much money they had donated.

But MSU released records of corporate ticket holders. The details shed light on the complex give-and-take that bestows coveted seats to a chosen few.

Coca-Cola, which pays $600,000 a year to sponsor Spartans sports, has 42 season tickets. EDS has 30 tickets. Meijer has 24. Summit Holdings Ltd. Partnership, an East Lansing real estate developer, has 20. Michigan National Bank, 16; Weyco Inc. of Okemos, 14; Comerica Bank, 12. The list goes on.

In fact, school records obtained under the Michigan Freedom of Information Act show nearly 40 companies and organizations had eight or more tickets this season to Spartans basketball.

The office of Gov. Engler, an MSU alumnus, got eight tickets, in addition to the undisclosed number Engler buys personally.

Not all of the tickets held by companies were for great seats, but many were. Lower bowl seats, the best in the house, have a face value of $20 per ticket. Season tickets run $324 each. But the real price often is far more than face value.

As in other big-time athletic programs, some of the best MSU tickets go to people and companies with business contracts and sponsorship deals. Other great seats are assigned through a scoring system that rewards contributions and other gestures of support.

The higher the points a buyer has, the better the seats.

Loyal fans get six points for every year of continuous season ticket purchase. They get one point for every $100 given to the athletic department. One point comes with every $500 donated elsewhere in the university.

More points are awarded for things like deferred gifts, memberships in school clubs and whether the buyer is an employee or alumnus.

GET YOUR TICKETS

About 8,000 tickets remained Wednesday for the NCAA Tournament South Regional's first- and second-round games in Memphis, Tenn.

The No. 1 seed Michigan State Spartans play at 7:40 p.m. Friday against first-round opponent Alabama State.

Tickets can be purchased in two packages. Six games cost $120. Two-game packages are available for $40.

Call the University of Memphis Ticket Office at 901-678-2331, 9 a.m.-5:30 p.m. weekdays.

Tickets cannot be purchased through Michigan State University, said Mark Hollis, associate athletic director.

If the Spartans advance to next week's games in Atlanta, the school will be allotted 1,250 tickets for each game. Priority sales will go to donors of at least $1,000 to the MSU Athletic Department, Hollis said.

But some tickets are expected to be available for students.

Hollis said details will be announced Monday if the Spartans advance.

EDS has bought its 30 tickets since 1985 and uses them as employee rewards. Meijer, with its 24 tickets, sponsors athletic programs. Summit Holdings, with 20 tickets, contributes $2,000 a year to the athletic department, and has donated an extra $50,000 to $100,000 over time elsewhere in the university, according to a general partner.

McKay Properties, a Battle Creek business with tickets since 1960, gives $10,000 or more yearly to the athletic department. The company has 12 seats.

J.B. McKay, a co-owner, said he feels for those who can't get in to watch the Spartans, but "if you're a donor, you should be able to get them, too -- especially paying that much. If I was some yahoo who never paid a cent to the school, I'd say give them to the students."

School sports officials said big-budget athletic departments, which generally support themselves, have to do business this way.

"The athletic department has to generate dollars to keep itself going," said Mark Hollis, associate MSU athletic director who oversees sports fund-raising. "It's something you don't want to do, but at the same time you want to reward people who are helping you meet your budget."

Such fund-raising is growing in major college sports, especially as athletic departments struggle with the rising costs of adding sports, improving aging facilities and paying more for travel.

Heading skyward

One result is ticket prices go far beyond face value.

"First, it happened in pro sports. Who can afford to go to a hockey game any more, or even baseball?" asked Dan Fulks, a Transylvania University accounting professor who has analyzed school budgets for the National Collegiate Athletic Association. "And now that's working its way down to college athletics."

Every three years or so, MSU squeezes ticket holders even more. The school rounds up the tickets for the best basketball seats and recalculates everyone's points, including those earned from recent contributions. Then the tickets are redistributed. That keeps the cash flowing.

To keep good seats, Hollis said, most ticket buyers must give enough money to get in the top tier of donors and points.

And since nobody knows what other ticket buyers are willing to donate, it drives up contributions.

Donations to the athletic department rose dramatically this season, from an average of $2.5 million each of the previous four years to $4.2 million, Hollis said. Plus, profits from school paraphernalia spiked to $1.8 million from $900,000 a year earlier.

But the price of all this may be fewer seats for those who have less money to spend.

"I think there's more hope for the students because most universities realize there's a responsibility to the student," said Fulks, the accounting professor. "You and me, we're the ones who are going to get left out here."

There were no tickets available on game days this season, Hollis said. That left the general public out of the seat derby. Some students said they felt left out, too.

Hollis said every student who applied for a season ticket this season got one. But that doesn't count the hundreds who tried and failed to get seats in the Izzone, the courtside cheering section named after head coach Tom Izzo.

Departing seniors left 253 seats open in the section, but an estimated 900 people showed up two days early for the first-come, first-served tickets last fall.

Student codirectors of the cheering section, who distribute the tickets, were overwhelmed.

"Basically, we had to turn away like 700 people," said Brad Johanson, a student codirector of the Izzone. "Obviously they were upset."

Klink, the sophomore from St. Johns, was one of them.

"The way I look at it is, the students are the true fans," Klink said. "When you have a rockin' stadium, that really helps out your team."

Indeed, MSU has the longest home winning streak in the nation, at 44 games. The Izzone has been credited with ratcheting up pressure on opposing teams.

But Hollis said the only way more seats may become available for students or the general public next year is if some season tickets are given up voluntarily. There are no plans to reduce the large numbers held by companies and individuals.

Klink said she will be better prepared.

"I'm going to come three days before they go on sale."
