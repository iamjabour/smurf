Colorado Students Score Near Top In Math, Science
Generally, Eight-Graders Still Lag Behind 17 Other Countries In Math, Science
COLORADO SPRINGS, 11:53 p.m. MDT April 4, 2001 -- Eighth-graders in Colorado Springs can boast that they are among the best math and science thinkers for their age group because they are, according to the results from a international study released Wednesday.

The Academy School District No. 20 in Colorado Springs, Colo., scored near the top in science and well above the world average in math.

Alisabeth Hohn, the district's assessment director, said that it helps to have the U.S. Air Force Academy located within the school district.

"A lot of our students have opportunities to participate in activities that promote science and math achievement," she said. Several high-tech companies are also nearby, and most students have computers at home, Hohn added.

The study of 50,000 students is a project of Boston College and the International Association for the Evaluation of Educational Achievement, an independent international cooperative of national research institutions and government agencies.

The tests were given voluntarily in 1999 in 38 countries. In the U.S, 13 states and 14 school districts or groups took part. The eighth-graders who took the test were selected randomly.

School District No. 203 in Naperville, Ill., west of Chicago, edged out all other competitors in science and came in sixth worldwide in math. The average math scores of Naperville students were just behind those of students in five Asian nations.

As a group, U.S. eighth-graders tested just above average, lagging behind 18 other countries in math and 17 in science, even though research showed the typical American student enjoys many advantages -- such as college-educated parents and computers in the home -- unlike many students in several top-scoring Asian countries.

Boston College researcher Ina Mullis said that many top-performing nations, including Singapore, Taiwan and Hong Kong, compensate by putting students through a rigorous curriculum and preparing teachers well.

"They work hard on development," she said, noting that the results make many U.S. schools seem like "underachievers," given their advantages.

Among participating countries, five Asian nations -- Singapore, South Korea, Taiwan, Hong Kong and Japan -- scored above all others in math, while Taiwan, Singapore, Hungary, Japan and South Korea were tops in science, scoring just below the Naperville district but comparable to other high-scoring districts in the United States.

Researchers said that teachers with a degree in math or science helped raise student achievement in the Asian countries, but that in the United States students were more likely to be taught by teachers with degrees in education or in subjects other than math or science.

The report also suggested that students score higher in math when teachers emphasize reasoning and problem solving, and score higher in science when teachers emphasize experiments and practical investigations.
