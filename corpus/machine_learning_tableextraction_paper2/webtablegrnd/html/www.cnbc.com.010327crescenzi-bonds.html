Crescenzi: Recovery Ahead

By Tony Crescenzi

Chief Bond Market Strategist, Miller Tabak & Co., LLC, and CEO of Bondtalk.com



Back to Previous Page
Mar 27, 2001 04:30 PM



The jump in the Conference Board's consumer confidence index indicates that the economy is now on the path toward recovery and the source of that recovery could well be two old-economy sectors: automobiles and housing.

In and of itself, the confidence surge could help feed further improvements in consumer and investor confidence, as individuals will likely be heartened by media reports of the rise. So in a sense, even if the data are fluky, the impact of the report on individuals' psyche could be material.

  • MSNBC: Consumer confidence rebounds in March

The confidence spike fits with the economic forecast that I have been putting forward recently. In particular, I have argued that economic activity in the second quarter will be significantly boosted by the automobile sector, which right now is acting as a significant drag.

In the current quarter, the automobile sector probably subtracted as much as two percentage points or more from GDP, but it is likely to add one to 1.5 percentage points next quarter. This seismic shift will be quite palatable and will almost certainly be reflected in manufacturing statistics sometime by mid-quarter.

As the data improve, perceptions about the economy will likely improve, as well. Attention will then turn to the likelihood that corporate earnings in the second quarter will exceed current expectations when they are released in July. In turn, investors are likely to shift from bonds to stocks. Corporate bonds will likely outperform other fixed-income assets as well, with high-yield bonds performing particularly well -- as they did at the start of the year. Economically sensitive stocks and bonds are likely to outperform defensive names. Emerging markets will recover more of their losses.

Today's consumer confidence report gives an early glimpse of the potential impact that the automobile sector could have upon economic activity in the upcoming quarter. Specifically, the confidence jump appeared to be rooted in regions that are most affected by the automobile sector.

Consumer confidence had plunged in these regions recently, and the plunge was particularly acute in regions in which production cutbacks likely took effect. But with automobile production said to be up 10 percent sequentially in March versus February, consumer confidence in the affected regions rose sharply, ostensibly ion connection with rebounding auto production. Indeed, the Conference Board reported large increases in confidence in the north-central part of the nation, where a great deal of automobile production takes place.

Automobile production should rise sharply next quarter as stronger sales meet falling inventories. The combination of strong sales and recent production cutbacks has reduced automobile inventories to roughly 66 days of supply, compared to more than 90 days of supply in January.

Another factor contributing to the improvement in consumer confidence could be the recent drop in interest rates. As interest rates fall, millions of Americans receive a benefit in one way or another. Substantial numbers of homeowners, for example, have taken advantage of mortgage rates having dipped below 7 percent. This is putting much-needed cash in homeowners' pockets. Refinancing activity has been extremely strong so far this year, running six times higher than last year's pace. Moreover, low mortgage rates are helping millions of Americans buy new homes.

Still others are benefiting from reduced interest rates on their many debts. For example, more than 40 percent of home equity loans are tied to the prime rate, a rate that moves in lockstep with the Fed's interest rate cuts. In addition, small-business owners are benefiting, too, as more than 40 percent of commercial and industrial loans are tied to the prime rate.

In short, it appears that just when many investors and economists had turned their backs on the old economy, it is the old economy, led by the automobile sector, that appears most likely to put the overall economy back on the road again.
