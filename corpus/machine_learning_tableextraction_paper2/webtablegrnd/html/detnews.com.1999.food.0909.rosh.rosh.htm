Rosh Hashana brings joy and unusual fruits to the table



Julie Siegel

Aunt Dorothy?s Israeli Chicken is surrounded with fruit. Recipe .



By Beverly Levitt / Special to The Detroit News

The Jewish New Year begins at sundown Friday, marking the commencement of the 10 High Holy Days or Days of Awe, when Jews around the world are called upon to reexamine their lives ? to wake up and not only smell the roses, but plant them for other people to enjoy.

The Festival of Trumpets begins with the blowing of the ceremonial shofar (ram?s horn) on the first night of Rosh Hashana and ends with its final call ? one long note sounding eerily like a human cry ? at sundown on Sept. 20, at the end of Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement.

?The sounding of the shofar is a call to action ? making sure we perform deeds of loving kindness such as helping out at a homeless shelter or making a donation to a charity such as Mazon, which gives money to the poor,? says Rabbi Dannel Schwartz of Temple Shir Shalom in West Bloomfield. It?s also a time for repentance and reflection for the 96,000 Jews in Metro Detroit.

Rosh Hashana is called the birthday of humanity. ?It is said that when God created Adam and Eve, he did it on Rosh Hashana,? says Rabbi Avraham Jacobovitz of Congregation Machon L?Torah in Oak Park and the Jewish Resource Center in Ann Arbor. Many parents bake birthday cakes with their children. The children decorate the cake, place candles on it and give ?gifts? to the world, mostly about changing their behavior ? ?watering the trees near my house,? ?not fighting with my brother,? ?being more thoughtful of others.?

The joyous custom of eating sweets and unusual fruits at Rosh Hashana signifies the wish for a sweet New Year.

?I grew up in Israel and every Rosh Hashana my father would buy all kinds of exotic fruits,? says Rabbi Jacobovitz. ?When I first tasted pomegranates ? which are made entirely of juicy seeds ? he explained that the seeds symbolized the hope that our merits would be multiplied in the upcoming year. My kids can hardly wait for Rosh Hashana. They ask, ?Where is all the sweet stuff?? They?d like to have the holiday all year round,? he laughs.

On Rosh Hashana Jews dip apples and challah (ceremonial eggbread) into honey. Dried fruit, sugar and honey are used to sweeten traditional dishes including entrees, vegetables, salads and desserts. The challah is round instead of loaf-shaped to symbolize no beginning and no end. Raisins are added for additional sweetness.

At this time of year, many families use traditional recipes that have been handed down from generation to generation, from grandmother to mother to daughter, which makes this holiday more meaningful.
