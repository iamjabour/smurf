                                                  Forecasting equation:

                                                  c t = a 0 + a 1 * c t-1 + b 1 * z 1,t-1 + b 2 * z 2,t-1 + b n * z n,t-1

                                                  c= coincident index (annualized growth rate, 6-month span)

                                                  z =leading index or indicator (growth rate, 6-month span all series, except interest rate spread and index of consumer expectations, which use levels).

                                                  Sample period: 1960-1997, semi-annual frequency. All data as of November 3, 1998.

                                                  		R 2 = measure of in-sample correlation (squared) of predictions and actuals. FSE = Forecast Standard Error (inversely related to expected precision or accuracy).
                                                  Discussion of LEI Forecasts Based on data through September 1998 (as available November 4, 1998)

                                                  The first line in the table shows results from using the leading index along with the lagged dependent variable as explanatory variables. The R 2 value of .465 means almost one-half of the variation in the coincident index (measured by growth at a semi-annual frequency) can be explained by these two series. The "Sept-Mar 1999 Prediction" column shows the corresponding forecast is for the coincident index to growth at a 2.4 percent annual pace over the next six months. The table also includes measures of the variance-from-actual that will typically be observed with these types of forecasts. For the leading index equation, the expected error (FSE) is 2.2 percent—which is based on both the historical fit of the estimated equations and recognition that the coefficients are surely estimated with some error.
                                                  Because the implied level of precision is not very high, it would not be unusual for the actual growth rate over the next six months to differ by up to 2.2 percent from the point estimate. Under the assumption of normally distributed errors, the probability of a negative growth rate is approximately 15 percent and about 65 percent of the time the actual value would fall between 0.2 percent and 4.6 percent.
                                                  The next 10 lines in Table 1 show results from using the leading index’s 10 components in the same forecasting equation framework. This exercise allows us to compare the predictive power of the individual indicators since 1960 (interest rate spread has the highest R 2 , while average weekly hours has the lowest R 2 ) and to compare their current signals about growth (money supply predicts the highest growth, while interest rate spread predicts the lowest growth). It is important to note that in all cases, the corresponding expected forecast errors are large, such that differences in the point estimates are not statistically significant.

                                                  The overall results show that growth prospects for the economy have cooled noticeably over the past six months. Based on this forecasting equation approach, data through March 1998 yielded a point estimate of around 3.5 percent and a prediction that growth in the “2 to 4.5 percent range for the March—September 1998 span has a 65 percent chance of being correct” (see Leading Indicators and the Prospects for Growth in 1998, June 1998.) The latest data shows the coincident index rising at a 2.2 percent annual pace for the six months through September, such that actual growth fell on the low side of this range. The results above show most indicators favor a continuation of the slowdown (compared to growth of over 3.5 percent in 1997), with most point estimates at 2.6 percent or less. A full percentage point decrease in the expected range, to 1-3.5 percent, is reasonable.

                                                  While this forecasting approach is straightforward and objective because it is based solely on quantifiable data, it has limitations. First, a long sample is used and concentrating on more recent data (i.e., ignoring data before 1984) tends to lower the point estimates by .1 to .3 percentage points and cut the associated standard errors by almost a full percentage point. Second, a better than 50 percent R 2 fit can be achieved by using a subset of the indicators. Depending on the set of explanatory variables and sample period, the forecasts vary greatly. Third, additional factors such as the current low unemployment rate, declining commodity prices, and tumultuous international conditions are not explicitly considered. These points would cause most forecasters to use this type of information as only one of many available tools for determining the likely path of the economy.
