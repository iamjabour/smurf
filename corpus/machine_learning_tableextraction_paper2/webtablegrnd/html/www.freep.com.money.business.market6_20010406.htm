Stock panic gives way to hope

Experts debate if Thursday rally was oasis or mirage

April 6, 2001

BY TOM WALKER

COX NEWS SERVICE

The start of something big, or a bear trap?

Whether Thursday's stunning $500-billion stock market gain was the real thing or a false rally is the question that lingers for investors.

DOW'S BIGGEST DAYS

The Dow Jones Industrial Average recorded its second-largest point gain on Thursday, but it was not a record percentage gain. The 10 best point days for the average, including percentage change in value:

March 16, 2000 -- 499.19 to 10,630.60, 4.9 percent.

April 5, 2001 -- 402.63 to 9918.05, 4.2 percent

Sept. 8, 1998 -- 380.53 to 8020.78, 5.0 percent

Dec. 5, 2000 -- 338.62 to 10,898.72, 3.2 percent

Oct. 28, 1997 -- 337.17 to 7498.32, 4.7 percent

Oct. 15, 1998 -- 330.58 to 8299.36, 4.1 percent

March 15, 2000 -- 320.17 to 10,131.41, 3.3 percent

April 3, 2000 -- 300.01 to 11,221.93, 2.8 percent

Jan. 3, 2001 -- 299.60 to 10,945.75, 2.8 percent

Sept. 1, 1998 -- 288.36 to 7827.43, 3.8 percent

Analysts attributed the rally to Dell Computer 's affirmation that it would meet its first-quarter sales and profit forecasts.

But strategists said that was a trigger mechanism at best. Stocks had become hugely oversold in recent weeks and were primed for a rebound.

The immediate response by many analysts was that one day doesn't make a trend and it's too early to tell if the yearlong bear market has finally bottomed.

"I wouldn't get your hopes up that the current buying frenzy can be sustained for more than a day or two," said Bernie Schaeffer, chairman of Schaeffer's Investment Research .

Alfred Goldman, strategist for A.G. Edwards & Sons, said the market has been bottoming "for the past month."

"Whether this is just a trading rally or the resumption of the bull market remains to be seen," he said.

Margaret Popper, an analyst at Standard & Poor's, said investors, lost "in the wind tunnel of first-quarter earnings reports," respond to any good news.

"Indeed, the powerful rally on Thursday reflects that the mood on Wall Street may be starting to shift to the view that quarterly earnings declines will stop with the end of the first half of 2001," she said.

Given Wall Street's penchant for treating bad economic news as good investment news, some analysts said a slight uptick in today's March unemployment report would probably keep the rally going, since it would indicate another interest rate cut by the Federal Reserve.

And that may happen, if a Bloomberg News poll of analysts is correct. The consensus forecast is that the March jobless rate will rise to 4.3 percent from 4.2 percent in February -- still low by historical standards, but trending higher.

The economists anticipate only 60,000 new non-farm jobs in March vs. 135,000 a month earlier. The Federal Reserve has already lowered rates three times this year for a total of 1.5 percentage points, and indicated each time a willingness to lower rates further if needed.

Thanks to Dell, buyers were attracted to a broad range of tech stocks. Intel was the top gainer on the Dow Jones industrial average, followed by Hewlett-Packard , Microsoft , AT&T and IBM .

The Dow surged 402.63 points, or 4.2 percent, to 9918.05, its second-biggest one-day point gain ever, behind the 499.19 points added March 16, 2000. The broader Standard & Poor's 500-stock index posted its best day in three months, rising 48.19 points, or 4.4 percent, to 1151.44.

The Nasdaq composite index also had its best day in three months as it climbed 146.20 points, or 8.9 percent, to 1785.00.

Analysts say the big test for individual investors probably lies in the next three to six months.

"Investors will have to get through first-quarter earnings announcements as best they can," said Standard & Poor's Popper. "That spells volatility, even if there's value at current levels."
