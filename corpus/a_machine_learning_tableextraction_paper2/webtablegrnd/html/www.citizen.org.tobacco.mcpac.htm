Senate Republican Leaders Kill Tobacco Bill: Hooked on Cash Crop

What Senator Lott and his colleagues have done today is public health malpractice, plain and simple. Ignoring the advice of every health professional in America, they have chosen to listen only to a handful of television ads and a lot of PAC committees. I hope that the Senators who derailed this bill today lose sleep every night listening to the sound of children taking their first puff and the sound of emphysema and cancer patients gasping for their last breath.

-- Dr. C. Everett Koop, June 17, 1998.

Despite bipartisan Congressional support, the approval of the public health community, and widespread public interest, Senate Majority Leader Trent Lott and the Republican leadership decided to thwart efforts to pass national tobacco-control legislation. Why? For years, many Republican Senators and the national Republican Party have taken millions in PAC dollars and soft money contributions from Big Tobacco -- nearly 5 times as much as their Democratic Party counterparts. Though the industry's proposed sweetheart deal was eventually transformed into a strong public health bill, the Republican leadership managed to pay back the tobacco companies by crushing the legislation Big Tobacco feared most.

In order to reveal the connection between tobacco contributions and pro-tobacco votes, Public Citizen analyzed 14 recent Senate votes on the McCain tobacco bill (S. 1415). Senators were given credit for pro-consumer votes (votes against the Big Tobacco position) and no credit for pro-tobacco votes. Senators voting "Present" or not voting were neither given credit nor penalized for their votes. (A 0% score represents 14 votes for the Big Tobacco position, while a 100% score represents a consistent pro-consumer vote record.)

PAC Money/Votes Summary

Public Citizen correlated pro-consumer scores for the votes on S. 1415 with the amounts received from tobacco PACs from 1993-1997 (the last three election cycles). The analysis shows that those 34 Senators who voted most of the time with Big Tobacco (scores of 0% - 20%) received over 7 times the amount of tobacco PAC money on average than the 40 who voted with consumers most of the time (scores of 80-100%). Senators voting with Big Tobacco most of the time received an average of $22,049 in tobacco contributions from 1993-1997, while Senators voting against Big Tobacco most of the time received an average of $3,195 . The 19 Senators voting with consumers on every vote (score = 100%) received an average of $2,911.

Current Senators took a total of $1.12 million, an average of $11,195 per Senator, from tobacco PACs in the last three election cycles (1993-1997). Republicans collected a total of $922,000 (82%), an average of $16,768 per Republican Senator. Democrats took a total of $197,000 (18%), an average of $4,383 per Democratic Senator.

Soft Money

Since 1987, the tobacco industry has contributed $16 million in soft money contributions to the two national political parties. These contributions overwhelmingly favored the Republican national party committees, which received $13 million (81%) compared to the Democratic national party committees, which received $3 million (19%).

In 1997 alone, the tobacco industry contributed $3.18 million in soft money to the national political parties. More than $2.64 million (83%) of these soft money contributions went to Republicans, while Democrats received $541,000 (17%).

Included Votes *

All four cloture votes and most of the major amendments to S. 1415 were included in our analysis. Certain amendments were not included because they were on virtually identical issues as votes already analyzed or because they did not affect the tobacco-control programs in the underlying bill. A list and brief explanation of the votes follows:

1. S. Roll Call # 142: vote to table Faircloth amendment to impose a $250 per/hr limit on the amount of fees that attorneys can collect in State-based and class action cases against the tobacco industry. Pro-consumer vote = Yes

2. S. Roll Call #143: vote to table Ashcroft amendment to remove all the revenue provisions of the underlying bill. Pro-consumer vote = Yes

3. S. Roll Call #144: vote to table Kennedy amendment to increase the per pack tax from $1.10 to $1.50. Pro-consumer vote = No

4. S. Roll Call #145: vote to table Gregg-Leahy amendment to remove the liability provisions from the underlying bill. Pro-consumer vote = No

5. S. Roll Call #149: vote to table Durbin-DeWine amendment to strengthen look-back provisions in the underlying bill. Pro-consumer vote = No

6. S. Roll Call #150: vote to end debate and vote on the underlying bill, S. 1415. Pro-consumer vote = Yes

7. S. Roll Call # 151: vote to adopt the Coverdell amendment which removes money from tobacco control programs funded under the underlying bill to provide additional resources to combat illegal drugs. Pro-consumer vote = No

8. S. Roll Call #153: second vote to end debate. Pro-consumer vote = Yes

9. S. Roll Call #154: vote to table the Gramm amendment which removes money from the tobacco control programs funded under the underlying bill to pay for eliminating the marriage penalty for couples with incomes under $50,000 and to provide a tax deduction for health insurance for the self-employed. Pro-consumer vote = Yes

10. S. Roll Call #156: third vote to end debate. Pro-consumer vote = Yes

11. S. Roll Call #159: vote to table the Reed Amendment to disallow tobacco tax deductions unless certain requirements are met regarding advertising aimed at under-age children. Pro-consumer vote = No

12. S. Roll Call #160: vote to adopt the Gorton Amendment to limit attorneys' fees to a maximum of $500-$4000 per hour. Pro-consumer vote = No

13. S. Roll Call #161: fourth vote to end debate. Pro-consumer vote = Yes

14. S. Roll Call #162: vote on a motion to waive the Congressional Budget Act of 1974 with respect to S. 1415; failure of this motion would cause the bill to be recommitted to the Commerce, Science and Transportation committee. Pro-consumer vote = Yes
