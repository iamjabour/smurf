How to score scarce tickets

April 1, 2001

Opera is booming all over the country, but the hottest ticket is still one at New York's Met. So how do you get in if the performance is officially sold out? You have two options.

  • If you take the grand tour

  • Standing room

    Every Saturday morning at 10 during the opera season (January-April and September-December), opera lovers line up at the box office in the Metropolitan Opera House to buy standing-room tickets. If you can stay on your feet for three hours, this is a cheap option. You get a reserved place at the back of the main floor with a 2-foot length of brass rail to lean against, for just $12-$16.

  • Buying from a scalper

    This takes daring and a more flexible budget, but we've had good luck this way. If you're serious about getting in, for every ticket you want to buy, have about $150 in $10 or $20 bills in your pocket, firmly gripped in your hand. Don't take your money out until you've struck a deal, and don't show more money than you intend to hand over to the seller.

    Arrive a good half-hour before curtain time at the plaza at Lincoln Center in front of the Metropolitan Opera House. Put on your tough New York face. Before any sold-out performance, you will be accosted by people trying to buy and sell tickets. Especially keep an eye out for the guy in the business suit who has to get home to Long Island for his 6-year-old daughter's birthday party or back to the office for a special project, and who will unload his tickets at a loss.

    In general, you'll end up paying in the neighborhood of face value. Oh, and double-check the time and date on the tickets to make sure it's for the right performance before handing over your money.

    Note: Legally, a ticket can't be resold for more than $5 over face value, which must appear on the ticket. Also, a receipt must be presented if the buyer asks to see it.

    By Wayne Lee Gay
