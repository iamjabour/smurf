International Investing
The Case for International Bonds

Much like investing in foreign equities, exposure to foreign bonds increases your investment opportunities and, as a result, may enhance your overall return. Today, foreign bond markets represent more than two-thirds of the available fixed-income investment opportunities worldwide, compared with less than one-third more than a decade ago, as shown below. This portion is likely to increase as emerging economies issue debt at attractive yields to finance their growing infrastructure and expanding businesses. Adding foreign bonds to a U.S. fixed-income portfolio increases the chance that a portion of your assets will be exposed to the best-performing markets.

Diversification

While investing in foreign bonds may increase your return potential, they generally do not significantly increase the volatility of your portfolio over the long term. Indeed, adding foreign bonds to a domestic bond portfolio may reduce overall risk. That’s because interest rates in foreign countries frequently do not move in tandem with U.S. rates. The direction of interest rates is an important factor in fixed-income investing because bond prices fall when interest rates rise and vice versa. Although some individual foreign bond markets may at any time move in the same direction as the U.S., longer-term correlations are low. Therefore, spreading your assets across various markets can help smooth out the volatility of your overall portfolio as strong returns from some markets buffer weaker ones from poorly performing markets.

The chart below compares the correlations of various foreign bond markets with the U.S. bond market using J.P. Morgan foreign country returns and the Lehman Brothers U.S. Aggregate Index for U.S. returns. A correlation of 1.0 (or 100%) means that a country’s bond market always moves in the same direction as the U.S. market. A correlation of zero means there is no relationship between the movement of a country’s bond market and the U.S. market. Australian government bonds, for instance, would have provided the greatest amount of diversification because they had the lowest correlation with U.S. bonds from 1986 to 1999.

While adding foreign bonds to your portfolio may help increase your return potential over the long term, several factors may influence the day-to-day volatility in your international portfolio. These include the potential for greater political and economic instability in some countries, especially in emerging markets, and currency translation. In addition, political or economic upheaval could disrupt local bond markets, so investors must continually monitor and interpret the internal developments of countries. This risk can be greatly reduced, though not eliminated, by investing in government bonds of developed nations.

Currency Fluctuations

The aspect of foreign bond investing that probably generates the greatest concern is the effect of currency fluctuations because currency changes represent a greater portion of foreign bond than foreign stock returns. Therefore, significant changes in the value of the dollar relative to foreign currencies are likely to have a bigger impact, positive or negative, on international bond holdings than on an international stock portfolio. The value of the dollar relative to foreign currencies fluctuates constantly depending on interest rate levels, each currency's supply and demand situation, and other factors.

Currency movements can increase or decrease a foreign bond’s value in U.S. dollars (and its investment return), even if its price remains unchanged, because foreign earnings must be converted to dollars. Historically, foreign bond managers have taken advantage of the differences in relative value between currencies around the world, particularly in European currencies, to produce a level of diversification in their portfolios. However, with the introduction of the euro, the ability to favor one currency over another is removed for the 11 countries that joined the European Monetary Union (EMU) in 1999. For U.S. investors, the level of diversification produced by investing in foreign bonds and currencies is affected by EMU, but not removed. The euro may be utilized to achieve foreign exposure and diversification. Furthermore, there are many countries that will continue to use their own currencies, and opportunities for currency diversification will remain in European countries that are not EMU members.

“Currency translation can have a positive or negative impact on a portfolio,” explains portfolio manager Peter Askew. “Depending on the type of fund, we may use hedging techniques to cushion the impact of potentially negative currency movements. U.S. investors should avoid trying to time the market to take advantage of currency fluctuations and, instead, look at the fundamental case. Many international bond markets have outperformed the U.S. market over time, even accounting for the impact of currency movements.”
