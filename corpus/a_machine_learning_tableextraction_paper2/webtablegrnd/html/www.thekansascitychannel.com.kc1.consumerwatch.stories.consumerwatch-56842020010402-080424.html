Keeping Score Regarding Credit Is Essential

Once-Unavailable Credit Scores Available Online

KANSAS CITY, Mo., 9:10 a.m. CDT April 2, 2001 -- If you've been denied a loan or a credit card, it's probably because of your credit score. It's something all of us have, but have never seen.

Credit bureaus have been keeping credit scores a secret. However, there are ways of getting credit scores.

It's a piece of information that three-quarters of all financial institutions use to decide your financial future. In other words, getting it and understanding it are critical for any consumer.

Most people know how to order a credit report. Understanding it, however, is something entirely different.

"When I first got my credit report, it was like reading something in a totally different language," consumer Denise Wade said. "I thought I had gotten something in Greek and I was frustrated by that."

In addition, Wade was without her FICO or credit score, which is computed by Fair, Isaac and Co. For years, credit bureaus kept scores a secret, but a new California law now requires bureaus to disclose them by July.

The companies have decided that rather than disclosing them in California, it's a much more lucrative venture to sell them nationwide.

Equifax is leading the movement by offering the score and personalized analysis of what it means for $12.95.

"This way, you prepare yourself ahead of time and you what your chances are of getting a loan," said financial expert Rudy Cavazos.

But to improve your score, you have to understand the top four reason codes that explain why your score isn't higher. The single most important factor is the proportion of your balance to your credit limit.

If this is a problem, it is recommended that you pay down your balance.

However, shifting or consolidating balances is not the way to do it. Nor is opening new accounts. By doing this, your FICO score will almost assuredly go down, experts say.

Another important reason for a low FICO score is the length of time an account has been established. Building a long credit history with the same company is a good way to build up a credit score.

Some people have too many accounts with balances, which can lower a score. Another reason for a low score is that there have been too many inquiries on someone's report in the past year.

Those inquiries are usually posted when you apply for credit.
