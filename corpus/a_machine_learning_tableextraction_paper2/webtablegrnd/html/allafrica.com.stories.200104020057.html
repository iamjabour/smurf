Broadcasting Regulations May Stymie Active Value's Plans

Business Day (Johannesburg)

April 2, 2001

> Posted to the web April 2, 2001

Greta Steyn

> Johannesburg

Broadcasting regulations limiting foreign ownership of an South Africa radio licence to 25% could throw a spanner in the works of UK-based Active Value's efforts to hasten change at Primedia.

Active Value, which has an aggressively interventionist style, has built up a stake of more than 34% in Primedia, the radio and cinema group.

Critics see Active Value as asset strippers who want to dismantle Primedia to realise value.

Michael Markovitz, legal adviser to the Independent Communications Authority of SA (Icasa), said at the weekend the foreign shareholding had come to the agency's attention. "Our monitoring and complaints department has written to Primedia to seek clarity."

Analysts said the licence for 702 Talk Radio, wholly owned by Primedia, posed a problem for the regulator and for Primedia, as Active Value effectively held more than 34% of the licence.

An analyst said the situation was complex, as the licensing regulations had not been drawn up with listed companies in mind. It was likely Primedia would respond to Icasa's letter by pointing out that the foreign shareholding had been acquired by hostile investors, and did not have its blessing.

Nevertheless, the contravention would somehow have to be resolved.

The need for a resolution could provide William Kirsh, Primedia's embattled CEO, with ammunition against Active Value. But the situation could backfire, as the onus is on Primedia to ensure the licensing conditions are met.

The hostility between the Kirsh consortium of shareholders and Active Value flared into open warfare on Friday.

The Mineworkers' Investment Company (MIC), part of the Kirsh voting pool, backed the Kirsh family in rejecting a list of demands from Active Value. The demands included appointing Brian Myerson and Julian Treger to Primedia's board, and appointing a new chair to replace Issy Kirsh, father of William.

In discussions between Active Value and other shareholders, it was suggested that Paul Nkuna, the chairman of MIC, take over Primedia's chairmanship, while Kirsh senior would have remained on the board.

But Nkuna threw a curve ball on Friday when he read an angrily worded statement from Primedia's board to journalists and analysts at a presentation on Primedia's interim results.

"Messers Treger and Myerson have publicly adopted a non constructive, antagonistic and hostile attitude towards the board. It would be detrimental to the business of Primedia, its subsidiaries and its management and employees to invite the continuation of the non- constructive hostilities into the boardroom," the statement said.

Mark Barnes, the Johannesburgbased investment banker who is working for Active Value and is also seeking a directorship, was surprised by the attack, saying it went against an agreement reached in discussions between the shareholder blocs two weeks ago.

Active Value may be compelled to call an extraordinary general meeting to change the board, Barnes said. He had expected changes to the board to be announced on Friday. While still holding out hope for an amicable resolution, Barnes said it may be that the matter had to be put to shareholders to vote.
