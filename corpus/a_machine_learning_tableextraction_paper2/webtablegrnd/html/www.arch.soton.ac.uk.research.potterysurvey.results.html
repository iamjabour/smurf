and Bibliography for England: A Gazetteer

Some preliminary results

A preliminary report on the Late Prehistoric Pottery Gazetteer was submitted to English Heritage at the end of March 1997. This documented not only the project's history and methodological development, but also demonstrated its potential through the generation of some initial summary statistics. The following tables highlight the diverse questions asked by the gazeeteer of the later prehistoric pottery collections and the potential of this database for asking user-specified questions of this resource. All summary statistics have been derived from queries written in Structured Query Language (SQL).

It is emphasised that these tables represent some of the preliminary results of the gazetteer, and that the data from which these are derived have yet to be finally checked.

Variety in Recovery Methods

The context of discovery is essential for understanding both the usefulness of the assemblage for answering specific questions, and also the the nature of the ceramic resource that is generated through different archaeological activities. Of particular interest was the impact of the 'recent' techniques of archaeological intervention, namely evaluations, watching briefs and pipeline investigations, upon the recovery of later prehistoric pottery collections, the highly variable and regionalised nature of which is demonstrated by the summary statistics provided below for the counties of Hampshire and Northamptonshire. The total number of collections accounted for (7,282) accounts for multiple recovery methods on some sites.

Application of Scientific Techniques

A variety of different scientific methods for investigating the absolute dating, accurate characterisation, provenancing and practical functions of later prehistoric pottery collections are available in this country. The table below illustrates the frequency of application of these various techniques to the published pottery collections included within the gazetteer.

Type of scientific report no. of collections % of published collections
radiocarbon dating 63 3.4
thermoluminescence dating 9 0.4
petrological analysis 101 5.4
heavy mineral analysis 4 0.2
X-ray flourescence spectroscopy 6 0.3
neutron activation analysis 3 0.2
residue analysis 5 0.3

Frequency of scientific reports associated with later prehistoric pottery collections

The survey discovered that all of these methods are under-utilised and require concerted effort of application in the future both on single-site collections and within a research framework of regional investigations for solving problems.

More information will become available when the preliminary report has been accepted.
