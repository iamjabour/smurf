Tyco grows internally and via acquisitions

Tyco International, a diversified manufacturer, is probably best known as a voracious acquirer. But the company also has a well-earned reputation as a lean operator, and internal revenue growth is strong. June-quarter earnings per share climbed 38% to $0.58, topping the consensus estimate by a penny. Sales rose 27% to $7.4 billion. Sales excluding acquisitions grew 17%. Tyco, a new Forecasts recommendation, is ranked "accumulate."

The telecommunications and electronics segment drove results in the quarter, with 29% growth in revenue excluding acquisitions. The segment manufactures such electronic components as connectors and printed circuit boards and owns an 86% stake in Tycom (NYSE: TCM) , an installer of undersea fiber-optic cable networks. Tycom plans to begin operating its own trans-Atlantic fiber-optic cable by year-end 2001 and a trans-Pacific cable by year-end 2002. Tyco sold a 14% Tycom stake to the public in July. Tyco also is preparing for an initial public offering this fall of semiconductor unit M/A-COM.

Other segments are reporting solid growth as well. The healthcare and specialty products segment (10% June-quarter internal revenue growth) is a maker of disposable medical devices and plastic products. The fire and security services unit (12%) makes such products as fire sprinklers and provides electronic security services principally through its ADT operations. Flow-control products (8%) include pipes, fittings, valves, and other products.

Tyco only makes acquisitions that are immediately accretive to per-share profits. Major acquisitions announced or completed over the past 12 months include electronic-component makers Raychem and Siemens Electromechanical Components, the electronics business of Thomas & Betts, surgical-supplies maker General Surgical Innovations, fire-sprinkler maker Central Sprinkler, cable manufacturer AFC Cable Systems, printed circuit board maker Praegitzer Industries, and medical-products maker Mallinckrodt.

Tyco?s shares dropped nearly 60% last year when a newsletter published by a money-management firm that often shorts stocks questioned the way the company books charges and reserves for acquisitions. Tyco?s stock has since recovered and rallied to an all-time high, helped by an informal Securities and Exchange Commission inquiry that exonerated Tyco?s accounting practices.

In July, SEC staff ended their inquiry into Tyco?s accounting without taking any enforcement action. The SEC requested that Tyco move $0.02 per share in non-recurring charges from the December quarter into fiscal 1999, a minor adjustment that does not affect the underlying earning power of the company. More importantly, the SEC did not ask Tyco to change its accounting practices and left alone such key indicators as sales, cash flow, and earnings before non-recurring items.

Tyco is not cheap but seems reasonably priced at 25 times the consensus per-share profit estimate of $2.16 for the year ending September ?? or about 1.2 times the expected 21% annual profit growth over the next five years.
