Monday Paper survey results

tell it like it is

T hank-you to all those Monday Paper readers, who responded to our reader survey, conducted in May this year.

Broadly speaking, the majority of you think that your weekly staff publication is informative, that we should retain its name, despite circulation bottlenecks within certain departments (which means some get it on a Tuesday, others on a Wednesday, etc). Most of you also rate the quality of the newspaper as fair to good.

However, judging by some of your additional comments -- there were some hard-hitting opinions among them -- there is plenty of room for improvement. You also provided some good ideas for articles.

These results are especially important to the Department of Communication as they tell us where we are succeeding and where we are failing with Monday Paper . These will thus lay the basis for a new Monday Paper , to be launched later this semester. In order to maximise the two-way communication process, we welcome your letters and opinions on a variety of matters affecting, and reflecting, a University at work.

But back to the research findings: a total of 359 questionnaires was returned from 5 400 sent out (via Monday Paper ). These are some of the formal results:

  • Out of 358 respondents to the question of gender, 192, or 54%, were female and 166, or 46%, were male.
  • 143 of the respondents were academic staff members, 181 were administrative or support staff, and 63 were students.
  • Most age groups read Monday Paper , with fewer in the under 25 and over 55 groups.
    • Age groups 25 - 35 = 22%
    • Age groups 36 - 45 = 23%
    • Age groups 46 - 55 = 26%
  • Out of 352 responses to the question of home language the vast majority indicated that they spoke English (274 or 78%).
  • Most of Monday Paper 's readers have been with the University under 10 years:
    • 1 - 3 years 31%;
    • 4 - 10 years 29%.
  • 89% of readers said they received Monday Paper regularly. 44% said they read most articles, 27% read selected matter and 20% said they browsed through the newspaper.
  • Regarding the general quality of Monday Paper , most respondents thought it rated 3 (33%) or 4 (38%) out of 5. Readers' descriptions of Monday Paper ranged from informative (219), newsy (97), propaganda (83), and dull (65).
  • Those who had read certain articles rated them accordingly in terms of interest levels. The results appear above in Table 1.

As for the reasons for publishing Monday Paper , this is what respondents felt:

  • most agreed it was to keep UCT staff informed of issues and news (306 responses);
  • some agreed it was to highlight new academic developments (207 responses);
  • others said it was to communicate important research and/or research grants (167 responses);
  • a few said it was to waste money (18 responses).
  • Regarding the frequency of Monday Paper , of the 349 respondents to the question, the majority (244, or 70%) felt that the publication should continue to appear weekly.

  • 103 out of 358 respondents said they discarded Monday Paper after reading it, with an even spread of 85 each keeping, passing on or recycling it. 308, or 87%, of 353 respondents said that between one and three people read their copy of Monday Paper .
  • 111, or 35%, of 353 respondents said that Monday Paper rated 3 out of 5 when compared with publications from other organisations.
  • 222, or 66%, out of 334 respondents said they would not change the name of Monday Paper , despite circulation bottlenecks in some departments, while the vast majority (319, or 92%, of 345) of respondents said they preferred the A4 format (current format) for Monday Paper .

The following are some readers' responses to the question: What would you change if you were the editor?

  • Balance UCT's achievements with the challenges facing it;
  • Change it from a propaganda sheet to a newspaper;
  • Less personality cult-type articles talking about how wonderful our leaders are;
  • I would not like to stand in these shoes for all the money in the world!;
  • Come down to earth;
  • Content not humorous enough;
  • It's all a bit formal. Add some humour;
  • The Monday Paper reads like a party political pamphlet;
  • More debate/controversy;
  • The one-sided, positive, achievement-focused everything-is-for-the-best reportage. We need a balance to inspire credibility. Be less of a lapdog to the VC and Executive;
  • Improve entertainment section;
  • Less gratuitous image-building for UCT and executives;
  • More pages, more news, more ads;
  • I like the eclectic mix of news;
  • More humorous articles, perhaps a cartoon competition;
  • I would love some real debate and a variety of different perspectives on changes/developments at UCT; some different voices; many more letters and responses from readers;
  • Report more on the hidden people on campus. Strive to give a voice to the voiceless;

Look out for the new edition of Monday Paper on November 6, 2000. Ideas and suggestions for articles, guest writers, etc, are welcome. Send these to The Editor, Monday Paper , Department of Communication, Welgelegen, email monpaper@bremner.uct.ac.za .
