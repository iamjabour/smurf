AMD Makes First Score with Major Computer Maker

By Tim McDonald

March 5, 2001



AMD has scored a coup in securing its first major corporate customer, NEC, which will be using AMD chips in computers destined for sale in Western Europe.

Advanced Micro Devices (NYSE: AMD) announced Monday that it has landed its first corporate deal with a major computer maker, NEC Computers International (Nasdaq: NIPNY), for its Athlon and Duron processors.

NEC said it will sell its line of PowerMate DT business desktops, incorporating the AMD chips, to government and corporate customers in Europe. The computer maker will initially target France, Great Britain, Italy and the Netherlands.

AMD and others have been trying for years to break the stranglehold that Intel Corp. (INTC) has on the corporate personal computer chip market.

AMD 'Vital'

NEC, with headquarters in Japan, is ranked ninth in corporate PC market share in Western Europe, according to market research firm Dataquest, shipping 455,000 business computers in 2000. Although the company is ranked second in the consumer PC market in the region, it is fifth overall, with a 5.5 percent market share.

The company recently settled a dispute with U.S. rival Cray Inc. that could enable it to begin selling supercomputers in the United States for the first time in four years. In February, NEC downgraded its earnings outlook because of the slowdown in PC sales. The company hopes the AMD chip, which sells for less than Intel's Pentium III, will boost it's European business.

"AMD will be vital in growing our European business," said NEC's Dominique Bourrus.

Intel's Rivals Advance

Intel, the US$34 billion acknowledged global chip leader, has dominated the PC market for years -- and continued to dominate last year with more than an 82 percent market share, according to figures from Mercury Research. But, AMD has made inroads since the 1999 introduction of its high-end Athlon chip.

AMD increased its market share from 13.6 percent to 16.7 percent last year, largely on the strength of the Athlon, and increased its quarterly revenues from $250 million to $1 billion. One percent of the market is shared by newcomer Transmeta, with its Crusoe chip, and Cyrix Corp., which has lost ground since AMD's resurgence.

Though Athlon chips are generally cheaper than Intel's Pentium III, which offers similar performance, both computer manufacturers and customers have traditionally been reluctant to stray from proven Intel products. In addition, mixing processors and products increases costs.

Intel Losing Focus?

Analysts say the time could be ripe for Intel rivals to chip away at the company's 30-year dominance. Because of the slowing PC global market, Intel has been diversifying into consumer products and other areas. Some analysts believe that by straying from its core, chip-making business, the company risks losing focus -- and may lose customers, as well. Intel has also been hurt by several mistakes, including two recalls, as well as product delays and cancellations.

AMD picked up some gains last year, despite having its own share of manufacturing glitches and shortages. The company is aggressively pursuing Intel customers such as Gateway Inc., Compaq Computer, Sony and other major computer makers, which have signaled their willingness in recent years to deviate from using Intel processors across the board.

Also, as Intel phases out the Pentium III, it is pushing the Pentium 4 chip in the corporate desktop market. The chip can only work with Rambus memory, of which many customers and manufacturers are wary.
